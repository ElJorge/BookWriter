# Chapter 9 — Final

The wheel was moving now.

It came up out of the on-ramp's curve under his hands, the rim turning a quarter-turn left and then easing back, and the magnet on the door went lit, then dark, then lit — *HAYES HEATING & AIR, low and slow since '04* — as the oncoming headlights washed it and let it go and washed it again. The dead man's name, on the door, riding toward the place his name had been eaten to point at. *Magnet on. Bulb rolling.* The bulb rolled in the cup holder when the van took the camber. *Headlights, then dark, then headlights.* Cole drove at ten-and-two. The wheel had been still under the missing man's hands, then empty in the dark, and now it turned under his, and his were the pair that was going somewhere.

"In point six, the 210 east, stay right." Devang's voice came flat and exact out of the phone wedged in the cup holder beside the bulb, four hundred miles up the coast and somehow right at his ear. "There's an exhaust on every parking lot, Cole, every lot you've ever sat in. You're about to be on the one with his."

*Got it.* He didn't say it out loud. He moved into the right lane.

Beside him, Theo had his hands in his lap and then on his knees and then on the dispatch tablet, straightening it on its mount that didn't need straightening. "So I'm — okay, I'm thinking, if somebody — like if a security guy or whatever comes up, I'm not gonna say anything dumb, I'm just gonna — I figured I'm the second tech, or I'm — Cole, do I need a name? Like a fake one, or do I —" The flood reached the lip of the word and Cole said nothing, kept his eyes on the road, and the silence in the cab took the flood by the back of the neck and set it back down. Theo put his hands on his knees. "Okay. Yeah. Quiet van. Got it."

*Seat warm.* It still held the other man's shape, the cushion long and low the way a bed holds a sleeper. Cole sat in the warmth and drove the warmth toward the cold.

The mall came up out of the dark exactly the way Devang said it would.

Third dead box on the right. A row of stores with their signs unlit — a nail place, a tax place, a thing that used to be a sandwich shop — and the loading apron behind them, a wide black flat of old asphalt with a single dumpster and a roll-up door and the rooftop units up on their steel curbs against a sky that wasn't quite black. *Apron clear. One dumpster. Roll-up shut.* Cole pulled the van in nose-out, the way you park a thing you might need to leave in, and killed the lights but not the engine, and the dead mall did not look at them, because a HAYES van at an HVAC site was the most ordinary object in America. Cole filed it without a word.

He read the place like a delivery address. The mechanical room would be off the apron — there, the steel door, the conduit running up the wall to the roof. The way to the units was the steel ladder bolted to the back wall, cage and all. He read the lock. He read the dark behind the dumpster, and something in the dark behind the dumpster he didn't have a word for yet, so he didn't file it, he just kept it.

> marco: STEP 4 — mechanical room, steel door, russ's master key is on the ring in the glovebox, the ring with the orange tag. cole CONFIRM you have the ring

The ring was in the glovebox. The glovebox was past Theo. There wasn't time to ask and there wasn't a clean way to ask, so Cole looked at it, and Theo — who had spent a whole life behind a bar reading the thing a man needed a half-second before the man knew he needed it — Theo already had the glovebox open, already had the ring with the orange tag in his hand, and held it out across the warm cab.

For one second the ring hung between them. The man who'd thrown the knife and the man it was thrown at, in the cab of the missing man's van, the missing man's keys in the air over the gap that was always going to be there.

Their eyes met over the ring.

Neither said it. There was nothing to say that the work didn't say first, and the work needed the ring to change hands, so it did. Cole took it. Theo's hand stayed open a half-second in the empty air, then closed.

"Orange tag's the master, the little brass one's the roof hatch, he labels 'em with nail polish, don't ask, you good?" Theo said it fast, too much, the flood pouring over the unbearable thing the way water covers a stone. "I'm on your six, I'm the clipboard, I'm bored, I'm so bored, look at me, I'm a guy who'd rather be home —"

Cole pointed him at the door, flat-handed him to stay, and was already out of the van.

He went up the cage ladder at a courier's even pace, the ring in his fist with the brass keys taped quiet against his palm so they wouldn't sing, and he came over the lip of the roof onto the flat gravel field of it, the units lined up on their curbs like the hulls of small boats, and he stopped.

There was a light up there that shouldn't have been a work light. It was a phone, propped against a condenser, throwing a cold white square up onto a man's hands.

*Russ.* On the roof. Working.

He was crouched at the northeast unit with the access panel off and his hands inside it, the panel screws lined up on the gravel beside his knee in a neat row, the way a man lines them up when he means to put them back, when he's working a job, when it's just a job — and a courier reads a scene the way a courier reads a re-taped box, and Cole read it: the hands knew the work. The hands were good at the work. They moved inside the unit with the unhurried economy of a man who'd done this ten thousand times — a man doing his trade, clean and competent and calm, on a roof he'd been driven to, under a light somebody else had set, while a second man stood at the edge of the gravel with his hands in his coat pockets saying something low and even that Cole couldn't hear and didn't need to hear, because he could see Russ's head tip a quarter-inch toward it the way you tip your head toward a dispatcher. The kidnapping looked like a dispatch. The work order had a man inside it doing the work.

Cole went very still.

It was the only tell. The man who never stopped, stopped — a half-second, the gravel under his boots not moving, the keys quiet in his fist — and he watched his friend's hands do good work under another man's clock, and he did not narrate it, because there was nothing in him built to narrate it, there was only the seeing, clean and complete and the worst delivery of his life. *The panel's off. The screws are in a row.* And then Russ's right hand, reaching for a screw, paused. Held a half-inch over the gravel. Held a beat too long over the neat little row he'd made — held there the way a man holds a true word in his mouth before he eats it — and then took the screw and turned it, smooth, like nothing, the seam closed over the top.

Cole filed it. Then he moved.

He could get close.

He came off the line of units low, the gravel taking his weight without a sound, the courier's even pace gone to something slower and flatter, and he crossed the roof toward the northeast unit with the cold square of light, and the man with his hands in his coat pockets was facing the other way, out toward the dead frontage, and Russ was crouched at the panel with his back three-quarters to the dark Cole was coming out of, and the distance closed.

Ten feet. Six. A reach.

This was the closest the world had let them get since the wheel went still. Cole came up on his friend's blind quarter with his hand starting to lift — not to grab, never to grab, just to land flat on a shoulder, the way you set a hand on a man to say *it's me, it's us, we came* — the hand of a man who had spent his whole life finishing deliveries, opening on the last delivery, the one that was a person, and the person was a reach away and breathing and alive and good at his terrible work, and Cole reached.

And his eye, on the way to the shoulder, snagged.

*A light.* On the dispatch tablet face-up on the unit's housing — Russ's own tablet, propped where a working man props it — a light. *There wasn't a light.* The screen had been dark when he came over the lip; he'd read the roof and the screen had been dark; and now there was a notification glowing on it, a single line of white text on the black, fresh, and Cole's hand stopped a half-inch off his friend's shoulder, the courier's eye snagging on the thing that didn't fit the way it had snagged on a pulled bulb.

*Something moved.*

Thirty miles back, in the same half-second, Donna read Cole's last line and her two fingers went still on the curtain. *How close can you get.* He hadn't answered. The thread had gone quiet under her hand, the men all reaching toward the same point, her husband a hand-length from the man she'd sent, and she could not touch any of it, she could only hold the one word she'd promised them, and her breath — the most controlled thing in the room — went, for half a beat, very slightly out of true.

She put her hand flat on the dresser.

The hairline closed. She typed.

The notification on the tablet held its one line of white text in the dark.

Cole read it the way he read everything. It was a message, dropped into the dispatch app, into the same app he'd read the address off of, into the open channel of the missing man's own work phone — and it was not from dispatch.

> *Mr. Hayes is needed elsewhere now. Thank you for getting him this far.*

And the man at the edge of the gravel, the man with his hands in his coat pockets, turned around — unhurried, courteous, frictionless, a man who had registered a variable he hadn't modeled and was simply adjusting for it, updating the ticket — and behind Cole the steel roof hatch he'd come up clear of opened, and Russ's hands went flat on the access panel and pushed up off his knees, rising, being raised, the dispatcher's quarter-inch tip of the head become the whole body coming to its feet because somewhere a low even voice had said it was time, and the screws stayed in their neat row on the gravel for no one.

Russ turned his head toward the dark Cole was standing in. His eyes found him. The bit came up on his face, warm and easy, the temperature of any disaster coming down a degree, *making it smaller,* the oldest thing he had — and his mouth opened around it, opened on the low slow shape of it, opened to eat one last word on purpose and smuggle one last thing out under the man holding the meter — "Hey. Hey, it's — fellas, it's nothing, it's a long job is all, you tell Donna I'm gonna fix that thing in the kitchen, you tell her low and sl—"

The hand closed on his arm and drew him back, and the word went with him.

Cole's hand was still out. His hand was still in the air, six inches, four, two from where the shoulder had been, the reach that had started on the wheel and crossed a county and come up a ladder hung in the dark with nothing under it, and the man it was for was already moving the other way, drawn back through the hatch by a hand that had been waiting, the way the bulb had been pulled, the way the light had come up on the screen — a step ahead the whole time, the cover that got them in the same name that got them seen, and the courteous voice came soft and even off the little speaker of the tablet, not raised, not gloating, a man closing a ticket on a variable he'd already handled:

"Thank you for the van. You can leave it. Drive saf—"

Cole's hand closed on the cold space where his friend had been.
