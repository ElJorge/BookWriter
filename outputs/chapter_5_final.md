# Chapter 5 — Mrs. Hayes

The cursor blinked under *it's—* and four thumbs did not know what to do with it.

It pulsed the way it had pulsed ten thousand mornings, the same patient little bar at the bottom of the same box, waiting to be filled the way it had always been filled — before the alarm, before the coffee, before the day made its first demand. Marco stood in the commuter lot with the phone in both hands and looked at the place where a name should have been, and for the first time in eleven years the box did not look like home. It looked like a window with the curtain pulled back. Russ's broken line sat at the bottom of it, *it's one of you, the one they'll reach first, the one who's exposed, it's,* and the white under it gave nothing back, and into that white Marco understood that whatever he typed to ask *which one* would land in a room with a fifth set of eyes, and the eyes would read it before any of his friends did.

The thread was the crime scene. The thread was the only way to say so.

> marco: Everyone. STOP. New rule, effective now — nobody types anything in this — wait.
> marco: I'm typing it in here. I'm telling you to stop using the room from inside the room.
> marco: Stand by. STAND BY. I have to think and there's nowhere to think that he isn't

The number didn't come. He'd reached for the next line — *1.* — the way his thumb always reached, and it stalled over the keyboard he had never once failed to fill, because the first item on any list now was *don't make the list,* and a list that began by forbidding itself was not a thing Marco's whole architecture knew how to build. He had spent ten years keeping this thread. He had pulled a man's address out of a decade of Christmas cards because he kept everything, because keeping it was the closest thing he had to being kept. And now the keep was the wound. Ten years of timestamps, lovingly filed — that was the map the stranger had been reading. *I built it,* Marco thought, standing very still in a lot where he had finally been allowed to move. *I built the thing he's been looking through. One timestamp at a time.*

> devang: ok if the endpoint's owned we — we have to —
> devang: hold on i can't say the next part

In a nursery four hundred miles north the word still wouldn't finish. *The chain is comp—* had hung undead in Devang's mouth for an hour, and now he had to act on the thing he could not name, and the next correct step stood up in him like a pager going off and he could not make himself press it.

And in the back of a Camry doing seventy-one in the carpool lane, the bit tried to fire and found no fuel.

> theo: ok so on a scale of one to
> theo: ...
> theo: i don't have the rest of that

The reflex went off the way it always went off — first, fast, older than thought — and reached the end of itself and found nothing there. There was no scale. There was just the white under Russ's name and a stranger reading over their shoulders, and Theo, who had a joke for the worst thing a person had ever heard, sat in a stranger's back seat and felt the armor fail to seal for the first time, a hairline, a draft coming through.

The shield had always been for outages — for things that happen *to* you, that you can work, that aren't anybody's fault. He could not stop. He went dark for the length of a held breath and then a line escaped him anyway, a status, a bit, a flare to the watcher that said *we're still here, still live, still findable* — because for Theo not-posting was a kind of death, the Chain was not a channel but an audience, and a man cannot stop being himself just because being himself is the hole in the hull. Every relapse was a great line and a continuing leak in the same keystroke.

Marco watched the read-receipts the way you watch a heart monitor. Each line he typed went gray-*Delivered* and then, a beat later, *Read* — and for ten years *Read* had meant a friend was there, and now *Read* meant a friend was there *and so was the other one,* and you could no longer tell the two apart. They were spending minutes they did not have failing to do the one thing, and the one thing was the thing that had let them save anyone for ten years, and to save Russ they would have to give up the only tool they had ever used to save him.

In the house the deputy handed Cole his phone back, and the handing-back was the worst part.

"Number where I can reach you." The deputy had the little notebook out, the radio muttering low on his shoulder, the last hour of a Saturday on his face. He wrote Cole's number in a deliberate hand, the way a man writes a thing into a record, and then he ran Cole's ID through the muttering radio, and Cole stood in the red wash listening to his own name go out over a frequency he could not hear the other end of, and the page in his fist said *no cops* in a dead man's living print, and the system the page had bled to keep out now knew his name, his number, and the address.

"Don't leave the area. We'll be in touch." Not unkind. That was the horror — the deputy believed him, let him go, did all of it gently, and the gentleness was the thing closing over the catastrophe like water over a stone. "Friend's welcome to wait for the wife. We'll have somebody back through." He clicked the pen. "Tell her to call us."

Then he was gone, the screen door tapping shut behind him, and the red light stopped turning, and Cole stood in a kitchen that had just been made official, holding a phone that had just become a leash. Free. Not taken. Named, recorded, and watched — worse than the cell, because the cell would have at least stopped him moving, and motion was the one thing Cole had. It had carried him a thousand silent miles. It had got him here before anyone knew he was gone. And now the motion was a thing the record could follow.

He looked at the phone. The Chain was in there, lit and screaming, buzzing against his palm. He did not open it. He had arrived, alone and cold, at the same conclusion three men four hundred miles away were failing at loud and together: he could not put any of this in the box. Not the runbook. Not the second cup. Not the chair angled at the door. The page in his hand was a thing that could only be carried, by a body, to a person — and there was exactly one person on earth it was written for, and she was not standing here, and Russ had said *call donna NOW,* and the call had cut on *who told you,* and the only move Cole had left was the one he'd never been good at, which was to finish the hard thing.

He got back behind the wheel of his own truck. And as he backed out of the driveway, down at the dead end of the street where the houses stopped agreeing with each other, a vehicle pulled away from the curb without lights and turned the far corner, unhurried, and was gone before Cole's mind could decide whether it had been a van.

The thread had been knifing itself for three minutes before anyone said the thing out loud.

It came in under the going-dark farce, the way a draft comes in under a door — Russ's *it's one of you* doing its quiet poison while the men argued about Signal, the four of them starting, against their own wills, hating themselves for it, to *read each other.* Marco had the whole archive, which meant Marco could see everything, which meant Marco was the most exposed surface and the most suspectable man in the room. Devang had found the breach, and the one who finds the breach is the one best placed to have made it. Theo posted everything, screenshotted everything, left his phone open on a hundred bars. And Cole — Cole was the one who'd gone dark, again, the silence that had been heroism reading now, to a frightened animal, as the silence of a man with something to hide. The paranoia was democratic: a friendship with total access had no alibis, and no walls, and so no way for any of them to prove the thing none of them could disprove.

And the panic needed somewhere to go, and it went, as it always went, to the mouth that turned every unbearable feeling into a bit.

> theo: i mean. ok. not to be THAT guy
> theo: but cole's the one who went dark and won't answer. again. maybe ask cole who he's been texting

In the white half-second after it sent, it was not a joke.

Theo felt it the way you feel the floor of a missed stair — the line gone, irretrievable, and for one half-second he meant it. He didn't mean to mean it. But Cole was dark and Cole wouldn't answer and Cole had gone silent before, and the frightened thing in Theo, who would lie down in traffic for Cole, who loved him stupidly and completely, had for one half-second looked at the empty space where Cole should have been and *wondered* — and the joke was just the shape the wondering came out in. The relapse-engine, which had not stopped firing all afternoon, used the joke as a knife for the first time, and the thing about a knife is you cannot un-throw it.

> theo: ...i'm kidding
> theo: that was a joke. that was a joke right

No one typed.

That was how they knew. The thread that had never gone more than minutes, the thread that had just spent three minutes unable to shut up, went silent — even Theo went silent, the relapse-engine itself stalling out, the dead air saying *something real just happened* louder than any line could have. And under the accusation, where Cole's name should have lit, there was a single gray word, and the word was *Read.* Cole's phone had it. Cole's phone had it and showed no reply — because Cole was in a truck on the 118 with no idea the thread existed anymore, being tried in absentia by men who loved him, in a room he could not enter, the silence that was *literally him protecting them* reading, to the watched receipt, as guilt. The accusation sat there, read, un-retracted, while a dot crawled west toward a motel and could not defend itself.

> devang: that's not — theo. the runbook doesn't mean one of us actually
> devang: i didn't mean treat each other like

But you cannot postmortem a wound between friends, and you cannot un-ring it, and the threat-model was in the room now, and what none of the four could hear — what only the white space could hold — was that Russ had almost certainly meant *the one they'll reach first,* the target, the soft one, *one of you is in danger,* and the panicked men had heard *one of you is the traitor,* and the gap between the two was wide enough to lose a friendship in.

Theo arrived in Simi at 3:14 in the afternoon, and there was no one there.

He had pictured it for forty-five miles — the arrival, the doing-something, the body in the place at last — and the Camry let him out on a sun-bleached residential street where nothing moved, where a sprinkler ticked over a lawn two houses down and a wind chime said one note and stopped, and Theo stood on the curb with his bag over his shoulder and felt for the bit the way you feel for a wall in the dark.

"Ok so the thing about Simi at three in the afternoon is—"

And there was no one to do it at.

A joke with no audience is not a joke. It is a man saying words to a parking lot. The setup hung in the empty air and found nothing to land on and fell, and Theo stood in a silence he could not perform his way out of for the first time in his life, because the phone was the stage and the Chain was the audience and both were gone — the Chain a wire he couldn't safely post to, the phone a thing in his hand with no one safe on the other end of it. And the feeling he had been outrunning since that morning finally caught up with him in a place with no stage left to run to. *I'm the closest one and I can't go,* he had typed in another life, and now the going was done, the want was granted, and being here was worse than being far, because far had a phone and an audience and a bit, and here had a body that did not know what to do with itself and a grief with absolutely nowhere to go. He stood in the wings of a stage that was not there. The smile-reflex went up at the corner and died half-made, the way it had died on a sidewalk in another life, except now it died everywhere, all of him, all at once.

Cole's phone rang while he was driving, and the number was one he half-knew.

He pulled to the shoulder before he answered, because the record was a leash and a named man does not take a strange call doing seventy. "Yeah."

A silence on the line that was not empty. A silence that was *reading* him. And then a voice, plain and clipped and held very flat, the voice of a woman who had put a phone-voice on over something else and was watching him through it. "Who is this."

He knew her then. The voice that had cut on *told,* an hour and a life ago. "Donna. It's — it's Cole. From the —" He stopped. The charm reached for the wheel out of pure habit, *hey, okay, I know how this sounds,* and there was nothing left of it; it came up hollow the way it had come up hollow for the deputy, and Cole let it go, and what was left was the only thing he had now, the small true sentence, wrong-shaped and costing everything. "I was at the house."

"You were at the house." She did not say it back as a fact. She said it back as a test, weighing it, turning it over for the seam. She was doing to him exactly what she had done to the message that moved her — reading his voice for the place it went wrong, *is this Cole or is this the thing wearing Cole's number,* because she knew now that the channel was a window and the window worked both ways, and any voice could be a coat. "What was on the table."

"A note." A breath. "Under the saltshaker. He moved the spare key to the meter box. The furnace filter — he said low and slow on the slide or you'll bend the frame." He heard himself saying the dead man's words in the dead man's cadence and could not stop, because they were the only proof he had that he was real. "It wasn't a goodbye, Donna. It was a — he was telling you what to do. He didn't lock the door. He left it open so you'd come in and read it and go to your sister's. To Carol's."

There was a sound on the line. Small. A grip changing.

Because *Carol's* was the seam in reverse — the name the fake had been missing, the name the lie had smoothed over into *your sister's,* and Cole had said it without being asked, the way only a man who'd held the real page in his real hand could say it, and for a half-second the wall she had stood behind her whole marriage came down, and she was not testing a stranger, she was a wife on a phone hearing a man she had never met be tender about her husband. *He always says low and slow.* They had him — these men, these names in her husband's phone, the boys who had taken his attention for a decade and whom she had only ever seen the back of — they had a Russ she recognized and a Russ she had not been given, the warm ribbed-on beloved one who got there first and made it smaller, and the unbearable thing was that the tenderness in this stranger's voice was real, and it was grief, and it was *Russ,* and for a half-second she let it be a comfort.

Then she took it back.

She heard the clamp come down in her own throat, the twenty-five-year reflex, *comfort is a thing that gets used against you,* and she filed the one moment of human contact she'd had since the floor went out under *possibly hostile,* because the man crying about her husband could still be the leak, or compromised, or the thing wearing his number, and she could not afford, could not ever afford, to let the alliance be a kindness. "Don't tell me what he meant," she said, flat again, the seam sealed. "Tell me what you saw. There were two cups."

He went still on the shoulder. "...Yeah. There were two cups."

"One was cold." Not a question. She had been there a hundred times in her head already. "Someone sat with him. Someone who wasn't in a hurry."

"The chair was pulled out toward the door." Cole's voice dropped. Two people with half the truth each — she had who Russ was, he had the data and the body — circling, neither able to trust the only ally they had, allying anyway because there was no one else. "Donna. I'm coming to you. I've got the page. I'm a — I'm forty minutes out. Where are—"

"No." Fast. "You don't say it on this line." She had learned the same lesson the men were dying to learn. "I'll —"
