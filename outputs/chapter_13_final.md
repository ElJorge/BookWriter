The deadbolt turned the wrong way under her thumb, snug, sealed, the way she liked a door. Then she turned it back.

She left it open a half-inch. Enough to hear the dresser drawer in the next room, the soft machinery of a sleeping child, the catch and release of breath that was not yet a word. The motel air conditioner ticked. Somewhere past the curtain the freeway laid its long sodium hum across the dark like a man humming to himself in a basement.

Donna laid the burners out on the nightstand in a fan. Four of them, plus the laptop Devang kept feeding her from two states away, plus the Gideon Bible she used as a mousepad because the desk was lacquered to a skating rink and the cheap optical mouse needed something with grain. A teething ring held down the printout. Blue rubber, gnawed pale at one end. She did not look at the drawer. Listening was enough.

Two acts, one door. Pull Russ out and the door he opened stays open; the job completes itself behind him. Close the job and the door seals with Russ on the inside of it. She inventoried it the way she'd inventory a pallet count. No knot in it. No room for a knot. A knot was a thing you could only afford with your hands free, and her hands were full of other people's.

The laptop chimed.

> marco: OK LISTEN. THREE WAYS THIS GOES WRONG —
> marco: 1) SCHEDULE TRIPS EARLY, DOOR LOCKS, WE'RE LATE
> marco: 2) COLLECTOR READS THE CHANNEL (HE READS THE CHANNEL)
> marco: 3) RUSS DOESN'T —
> marco: 4) —

The fourth item sat there with nothing after it. She read the dash and understood it was not a typo. It was the thing Marco's hands had stopped before typing.

> devang: site's dark. cams down, badge logs frozen, no PoE handshake, nothing.
> devang: i've got eyes on nothing. that IS the data, btw. dark is a value.
> devang: there's no API for a locked door. i keep — there's just no API for it.

In her ear, the live channel: Theo, breathing too fast against what sounded like cinderblock.

"Okay so I'm in the stairwell, the B stairwell, I think it's B, there's a little — there's a sticker that says B but somebody scratched it, and the card's, the card is right here, I have it, I have Russ's card, it's, my hand's a little — it's fine. I'm fine. I'm talking too much. Sorry. Stairwell B. Holding."

Then nothing from the fourth position. Not silence. A breath, drawn in through the wire and let back out, even, unhurried. A single tap against the mic housing. Cole, saying he was where he was supposed to be and that he had no use for saying it.

Donna took the roll the way she'd taken it off a loading dock at four in the morning for nineteen years. Marco. Devang. Theo. The breath. Present. All of them present who should have been home, who should have been anywhere but seven miles from a building that locked itself on a clock.

"Hold," she said. Minimum volume. "Everybody hold."

The new voice did not arrive. It was simply there, the way water is there, having entered the channel without a footstep.

"You've made this harder than it had to be," the collector said. No effort in it. No breath under the words, no room behind them. "But it isn't too late to make it simple again. You have a man inside. You can have him back. Pull him out the way he came and the door stays open, the count finishes on its own, and we're done. Or you can close it, and he stays where the work is. Either way you choose, and either way I help you. We'll correct it together."

Courteous. Reasonable. He sounded like a man returning a call about a warranty.

She heard the singular and kept it off her face, though there was no face to keep it from. *You.* One man. One choice. He was speaking to a single actor at the end of a single hand, because in his whole life he had never once had to imagine the other thing. He didn't know how many burners were fanned across a Bible in a room with a baby in it. He had never needed to.

She glanced at the fan of burners. She said nothing back.

The leakage started under the collector's voice, the way damp comes up under paint.

> russ: rows secure.
> russ: rows staged east wall. units accounted.
> russ: product ready for —
> russ:

The line ended without ending. She'd heard him do it for a year of bad nights now, this man she'd pulled off a dock and trusted with her whole crew, shrinking the thing he was looking at down to a word small enough to carry. *Rows. Units. Product.* And then the place where the real word should sit, and the eraser arriving on time, every time, and the gap.

> devang: wait.
> devang: the gap's not random. it lands in the same slot every time. same syntactic slot.
> devang: the thing he can't say has a SCHEMA. count, location, the noun he's deleting — it's structured. the null has a shape.
> marco: LOGGING ALL OF IT. TIMESTAMPING EVERY FRAGMENT.
> marco: 00:11:04 rows secure / 00:11:31 units staged / 00:11:58 product ready for —
> marco: i'm building the map out of the holes. the holes ARE the map.

A man she loved was disappearing himself in real time across the wire and Marco was writing it down like release notes, 00:11:58, the gap, version logged. It was the most useful thing anybody had done all night and it made her set the burner down very carefully so she wouldn't grip it.

She let herself have one.

"He doesn't get to be the one," she said. Quiet. Plain. "You hear me. Nobody on this channel is the one hand. There's no one here for the door to close on. You don't get to die for me twice, Russ, because there's no *me* to die for — there's *us*, and us doesn't fit in the trap."

She heard it leave her, heard it land, and then she sealed it back over, smooth, the foreman's face returning to the foreman's voice. "Theo. Breathe. Hold the card."

In her ear Theo had started to cry, the way relief cries, ugly and grateful and unable to stop. "Oh, okay, oh thank — okay, because I really thought, I thought it was gonna have to be one of us who — and I can't, you know I can't, last time I couldn't even — but if it's not, if nobody has to be the — I'm holding the card. I'm holding it. I'm breathing. Look, I'm breathing."

A machine would not have cried. That was the whole of it. The man in the channel who modeled people could model a hero, could model a coward, could model the math of one actor weighing one life against one job. He could not model a stairwell full of snot and gratitude that solved nothing and proved they were too many and too human to resolve into a target.

"Devang," Donna said. "Talk to me about the schedule."

> devang: i can't open the door. still can't. but i don't have to.
> devang: the door doesn't lock for a guard. it locks for a SCHEDULE. the schedule's a state machine and a state machine will believe anything you feed its inputs.
> devang: i can make the building think the job already finished. spoof completion. re-time the dark window so it reads DONE.
> marco: AND THE BADGE. the badge that reads as RUSS reads as THE JOB. one credential, one identity, the whole work order walks on Russ's card.
> marco: THEO HAS THE CARD. THEO IS THE JOB NOW.

"Theo," Donna said. "Your mouth. The thing that gets you in trouble. I need it. Talk the card through every reader like you own the building and you're bored of it."

"I — yeah. Yeah, okay, I can do bored, I can absolutely do bored, I do bored at the DMV, I —"

"Don't tell me. Do it. Cole."

A tap.

She conducted then, and she did not speak in sentences, because sentences were the one thing the man in the channel could take apart. She spoke in the cues she'd built with these people over a year of bad nights — a count, a name, half a word, a beat of silence held exactly as long as a held silence needed to be held. "Devang on my three. Marco, the eleven-oh-four pattern, feed Theo the door order. Theo, second reader, then go quiet, then the breach. Cole." Tap. "Now."

Across the channel the collector was getting noise. She could feel it. Fragments and taps and a man's eaten work-order leaking up through the floor of it, and nowhere in any of it a single person to address. Five small plans running at once, none of them sufficient, all of them necessary, cohering off-channel where a listener who parsed sentences could only hear weather.

She could not have said what to call the thing they were doing, and there was no time to name it. The schedule, re-timed, telling the building a fiction it was built to believe. The badge walking the work order down the corridor in Theo's sweating hand, reading as Russ, reading as *done*. And behind the lit, badged front of it, in the part of the building the schedule didn't watch because the schedule thought the job was finished, the cold room coming open under a pair of hands that didn't need to be told twice.

Make the door's completion *true* for the building and *empty* for the trap. Let the facility believe the job was done while the only thing that made "done" mean anything was carried out the back of it. Saving Russ could no longer complete the job, because completion had been a room, and the room was being emptied.

It came down, for ten seconds, to a card and a pair of hands.

"Cole, I can't get to the east stack, the reader on the east stack is past the line, it's in the lit part, it's past the — Russ's card won't reach, you have to be the one on the stack, I'll talk you every step but you have to *go*, you have to trust me on the count, I know — I *know* — just go on three, I'll have you, two, go —"

No answer in words. There would never be an answer in words from that position. Only the body doing the thing the mouth refused to make a ceremony of: Cole stepping across the line on Theo's count, on the word of the man on the other side of a knife neither of them had pulled out of the wall, both of them acting as one without forgiving each other a single inch of it. The trust bore the entire weight of the move and not one ounce of the wound. It held anyway, and stayed broken while it held — the way the door could be true and empty, both.

And to do it, to be on the stack in the lit part, a man stepped fully out of the dark and into the badged, watched, walled part of the building, and Donna heard it the way you hear a thing you can't take back: a step into the light, and somewhere behind him a door easing shut on the dark he'd been safe in. One of hers was no longer safe. She did not flinch on-channel. Flinching was a signal the listener could read.

The cold room came open under Cole's hands, and Russ broke.

> russ: rows clear. rows — east stack, the rows —
> russ: these aren't units, they're —
> russ:

The article gave out under him. The synonym that had held him upright all night buckled at the exact place the true word lived, because he was looking right at it now, at all of it, and the eraser that had carried him this far failed for the first time and the gap arrived and the gap was the most human thing the man had done in a year. The team read the hole. Nobody filled it. Nobody had to.

Because the hole told them what the access job had been *for*, and the floor of the whole night dropped out from under the binary. The schedule had never been a delivery. It was an erasure. Completion had always been the disappearance of the thing in that room, and the two opposite acts the collector had handed her — save him, stop it — collapsed into one, finally, the same act, if the move held.

The collector's voice came back into the channel, and for the first time all night it was not certain.

"The count is — " A pause that had no body to explain it. No breath, because he had never breathed, but the place where a breath would have been, empty. "The release was scheduled. The schedule shows — "

He stopped. He did not threaten, because there was nothing left to administer; he did not gloat, because he could not find the shape of the thing to gloat at. His whole function was to handle one actor, and the channel he was listening to would not resolve into one. His model returned null, and his mouth returned null with it, a frictionless man reaching for the next instruction and closing on nothing. He was vague. From him, vague was catastrophe.

Donna stood at the door. Her hand found the half-inch of open she'd chosen at the start of the night, the gap she'd left so the baby's breath could reach her, and she did not close it. Behind it the child slept on, indifferent, the stakes of everything made small and silent and warm.

The result started coming back in pieces.

> marco: SCHEDULE READS DONE. building thinks it's over.
> devang: dark window's closing. theo's clear of the readers. cole's —
> russ: russ is —

Hope spiked on the half-word and she would not let her face take it before the channel did. She kept her hand on the open door. She kept the foreman's face. Down the wire the man's status hung unfinished, *russ is*, and she breathed once around it, even, unhurried, and somewhere far off in the dark a lock turned that was not the deadbolt under her thumb and was nothing she had any hand on at all.

The collector spoke last.

"It seems you've all decided to —"
