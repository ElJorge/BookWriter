His hand was flat on the door before he decided to put it there.

Gloved palm, fingers spread, the heel of his thumb against cold steel — the read he'd done ten thousand times, the first thing the hands knew how to do before the rest of him caught up. You laid your hand on a unit and you let it tell you. A warm panel meant a fan was fighting. A hot seam meant a bearing going. Heat was the language; the metal always talked if you held still and listened low and slow, the way he'd done it since '04, the way Donna used to say he loved a compressor more than he loved a conversation.

The door told him nothing.

Cold all the way through. Cold past the glove, cold the way a thing is cold when nothing inside it is working — no fan, no fight, no fault to chase. There was nothing on the other side to f—

Nothing to service. Nothing on the other side to fix.

He took his hand back. Flexed it once.

The man-trap held him in its little throat of a room, two doors and him between them, and the badge reader by his shoulder showed a small steady green. Authorized. The log had his name in it, his real name, his company's real account number, the maintenance window opened clean and legal and his. On paper he wasn't breaking in. On paper he was the technician. On paper there was no crime here at all, and that was the part he kept setting down and picking back up like a tool too hot to hold.

A soft tone landed in his ear. Single note. Not a countdown — he'd learned that already, the hard way, in the first ten minutes. It didn't count toward anything. It marked what had already gone by. Elapsed. The window was running. He was inside the running of it.

"You're at the inner door." The voice in the earpiece. Quiet, even, no weight on any word. "Whenever you're ready, Russ."

Not *hurry.* Not *or else.* Whenever you're ready. The most patient voice he had ever heard, and he had heard a lot of patient voices, had *been* one — the flat reassuring hum a man uses on a frightened customer at midnight, *we'll get it sorted, ma'am, low and slow, no charge for the look.* That was the thing his stomach kept failing to swallow. The voice was his. It was his own bedside voice with the love drained out of it. Same temperature. Same unhurried kindness in the shape of the words. His calm cost him everything to hold; he could feel the price of it ticking under his sternum, a little knock, a bearing going. The voice's calm cost it nothing. It had no body to it at all. It was calm the way a closed valve is calm.

"Panel to your left," it said. "Lower right corner. You'll see the sequence."

His hand was already moving. That was the obscene part. His hands obeyed a half-second ahead of his gut, the way good hands do, the way you want them to when the work is honest — sure, unhurried, kind to the equipment. He found the panel. He found the lower right corner. He keyed the sequence the way you'd reset a controller for a customer, gentle on the buttons, no slamming, respect the machine.

He answered it like a dispatcher had asked him. "Got it. Running it now."

"Good."

That was all. No thank you he could have used against it. No cruelty he could have hated cleanly. He swallowed and nodded at a voice that couldn't see him nod.

—

The earpiece bled.

It did that — leaked the open burner Donna kept live, the team's channel thinning into his ear under the collector's clean line, the way a far station ghosts into a good one at the edge of its range. He wasn't supposed to be able to hear them. He could hear them.

*—no, but if you go over the top rail you want to roll, you don't drop, because if you drop flat you're gonna catch the* — Theo, too many words, the run-on of a man talking to keep his own teeth from chattering — *the bottom wire, it rolls under, I've seen guys, okay, I haven't seen guys, but logically* —

A chat line, flat in the bleed where Devang read it aloud:

> marco: STAND BY. FENCE BREACH — STEPS. 1) HANDS AT 10 AND 2. 2) WEIGHT ON THE POST NOT THE MESH. 3) DO NOT — DO NOT TOUCH THE TOP STRAND UNGLOVED —

*Marco,* Devang said, dry, *there's no API for a chain-link fence.*

A beat. Then, warmer than Russ had ever heard him, almost gentle: *I'd build you one if there was. I really would.*

And Russ — God help him — felt the corner of his mouth go, the old reflex, the laugh that meant *these are my people.* Devang would build an API for a fence. That was the funniest and truest thing anyone had said all night.

Then it curdled, fast, between one breath and the next, because the laugh told him exactly where they were: loud, lit up against the dark of a professional's running op, ten feet of chain-link and a numbered list between them and a thing none of them could see the edges of. They were climbing a fence with their hands while the door already stood open from the inside. They thought they were breaking in. He kept his hand moving on the panel so the tone in his ear wouldn't notice his hands had stopped, and he filed his friends, automatically, helplessly, as a job. *Loud crew, exposed, no plan that survives contact.* He hated that he could. He hated that the filing was a comfort.

*Cole's on the fence,* Theo said, lower now. *Cole's already over. How is he already over.*

Cole said nothing. Cole never said anything. That was the only quiet thing out there, and it was the only thing out there that scared the right people.

—

The inner door released.

It didn't swing. It exhaled — a man-trap door, a soft pneumatic give and a seal letting go — and the cold came out at him, not food-cold, not the wet meat-locker cold he'd been told to expect and half-believed, but a dry machine cold, a server cold, a hall of patient humming that had never held a side of beef in its life. He stepped to the lip of it.

Rows.

That was the first word his mind handed him and he took it, grateful, because *rows* was a word a man could hold. Rows of — units. Long bays in the cold, climate held tight, a manifest somewhere logging every one, inventory in deep storage, the spec maintained to the decimal because somebody paid to keep the spec exact. Product in a row. Somebody's product, somebody's records, somebody's long-term — and he made it to the end of the thought clean, the sentence scanned, the read came back *storage* and his hands stayed his hands.

Except his hand had risen halfway, the way it had risen to the door, the read-for-heat reflex — and it stopped in the air. Because the rows were not warm and not cold-and-empty. They were held. Held at a temperature you hold a thing at when the thing must not change, must not spoil, must not be allowed to —

*Spec,* he thought. *Held at spec. That's the job. That's all the job is.*

His breath went out of sync with the tone for the first time all night.

He stood at the lip of the cold and understood what his good hands were for. Not money. There'd never been money in it the way money is a reason. His credential, his clean account, his low-and-slow patience, the green light that said *authorized* — all of it had been spent to open this, to make legal access to the place where they kept the — the storage. The product. The units in their patient rows that someone, somewhere, wanted held exactly as they were and never, ever released.

He made it smaller. He stood there and made the worst thing he had ever seen smaller, because making it smaller was the only way to keep his hands, and keeping his hands was the only way to stay the man he needed to still be when this was over — and somewhere under the price ticking in his chest a single seam gave, one hot seam, and he heard himself breathe a thing that wasn't a word, that was just *oh.* Just that. Just the sound a man makes finding the fault.

Then he sealed it. Breathed it back flat. *Low and slow.* The seam closed.

"You see it," the voice said. No question in it. No hunger. "We'll correct it together."

—

The burner bled hard.

*—we're in, we're through, we're coming for him* — Theo, breaking up, breathless, exultant in the worst possible register — *Russ, hold on, we've got the line, we're coming IN* —

And Russ understood the geometry before any of them did, all at once, the way you understand a circuit when the schematic finally resolves: there was one way in. There was one door. It was the door he had just opened, the one that exhaled cold, the one his credential had unsealed and that would only stay unsealed while the window ran. They could not reach him except *through* it. To pull him out was to walk the access all the way open. To save him was to finish the job with their own hands and their own love and their own bodies coming through the breach. The rescue *was* the completion. There was no version where they got him and stopped it. Getting him *was* it.

"They're helping, Russ," the collector said, and there was no cruelty in it at all, which was the cruelty. "Let them."

He pressed his hand flat to the cold of the doorframe. Held it there. The knock under his sternum, the bearing going, hard now.

He keyed the burner. He had one move and it was the device they'd already turned against him, and he turned it now himself, on purpose, knowing he did it — the swallow he chose, not the swallow that chose him. He had to put a word inside a sentence and then take it out before the clean voice in his other ear could catch the shape of it.

"Don't come through the —" He ate it. Clean. Jaw shut on it mid-breath, the way he'd done it once before, out past Syl—, the word gone where a listener couldn't follow. "Pull back to the — you copy? Pull *back.* It's not a way in. It's the —"

He left the holes in it on purpose and prayed somebody read cold enough to fill them.

A pause on the line. Then Donna, relayed, thin and dry and absolutely level, the foreman's voice spent down to its last clean edge: *I heard you, Russ.* A breath. *I'm not burying you a second time.* And then it sealed — her voice went flat as a closed door, command again, no surface left showing: *Hold the line. Cole — door.*

*Cole's got it,* Theo said. *Cole — wait — what are you* —

—

The breach landed.

Not loud. That was the thing about Cole — nothing he did was loud. The outer seal gave on a credential it shouldn't have had, and Russ heard, in the bleed, Theo's hands fumbling at something, fast and useless, *I've got it, I've got it, no I don't, Cole, I can't get the —*, and then nothing from Cole, no word, no *here,* not even a *copy* — just the small definite sound, relayed, of an object changing hands. The keycard. Cole put it in Theo's hand. Theo's mouth was still going and Cole's was a shape that almost made a sound, the old held thing rising in his throat — *you don't spiral, you just* — and it died there, unsaid, and instead of the sentence he gave Theo the card, and the card was the sentence, and it worked, and it forgave nothing, and the door opened.

Cole came through first.

Russ's hand came off the cold frame and went out — flat palm, fingers spread, the heel of his thumb finding the body of the man through the breach — the read for heat, the same gesture, the same hand, the hand that had been on the door now on a man — and he felt warmth answer it. A living seam. A fault you couldn't fix. Cole, warm and real and *here,* the worst rescue, the door walked all the way open by the one quiet body sure enough to do it.

And in his ear the collector, even, unhurried, closing the ticket, began at last to name it — the first hard word for what the credential opened, what was kept in the cold, who the rows were, the noun the whole night had circled — "It's where they keep the —"

— and the job took the word.
