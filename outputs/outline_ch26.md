**POV:** Rhea Okonkwo-Dale, close third (her third POV; Ch17, Ch22). Plain-complete, dry; name forms clean.
**Genre lock:** thriller COMEDY — exhausted gallows humor + Rhea's dry plain observation kept live even in grief.
**Launches from Ch25 ending:** Russ at the cold-room door, many hands with him, the noun caught at his teeth, "this time he would not be the only hand on it." Ch26 opens at/just past that door swinging.

**Open/close rhyme object:** the COUNT. Opens on a number Rhea holds (the breath-count of bodies behind a door not yet opened / her lone signature once one entry, now one in a column) — closes transformed (the count of those KEPT against the count no chain reached; the same number now meaning a different thing through her perception). **Cold-room discipline:** the held people are NEVER named, not even as they are saved. The room STAYS FULL (the big inversion). Rendered only via euphemism, the room being full not empty, the crew's reaction, Russ's buckled article. The unsaid noun, for the first time, points at people who are still there.

## BEATS

**1. OPEN — the door, the many hands, the count Rhea is holding.**
Open inside Rhea at the threshold. The door comes off its seal — many hands on it (rendered from her outside vantage; Russ's among them, his grip the one she watches). Concrete, sensory: the seal's give, cold air rolling out, the over-loud breathing of seven people who climbed a long way to stand here. Establish the rhyme object: Rhea is counting — a habit from being plain-complete; she counts hands on the door (more than the one that opened every door before it), counts her own held breath, knows the number behind the door is a number that, every prior time, became zero. Both registers: the absurd relief (Theo whispering that they should have brought a battering ram, then realizing it was unlocked the whole time — gallows comedy) against the dread of opening onto another empty room.

**2. THE ROOM STAYS FULL — the mystery's emotional payoff (NEVER print the noun).**
The door opens on the room that, in every prior instance, "was full and then wasn't." THIS time it is still full. Render ENTIRELY via: the room not being empty; the crew going wordless; the buckled article on Russ. The people PRESENT — surfaced only as breath, as warmth where there should have been none, as a count that does not collapse to zero, as eyes turning toward the door. Land the inversion plainly through Rhea: every room in this pattern emptied; this one did not. The chain kept a room full. Russ's eaten-word: he tries to name what is in front of him, buckles at the article, the noun caught at his teeth — but for the first time the unsaid thing is alive, looking back. Hold the noun unsaid even as it breathes.

**3. RHEA'S MIRROR — the heart (no sentimentality).**
Rhea — herself a no-chain target the engine selected, of a kind with these people, who survived only because a chain reached her in time (Ch16–22, held Ch22) — looks into the full room and sees the fate she escaped. Plain-complete, dry: she does the arithmetic on herself — there was a version of this room with her in it, and the only difference between her and the count behind that door is timing and a chain of strangers who reached her. Now she is a hand on the door; the held became the holder. Render the recognition as a fact she states to herself, not a feeling she indulges. The chainless became a chain. One landing only — no wallowing.

**4. THE GRIEF / THE COST — the win is not clean.**
Stopped, not undone. The prior rooms — the industrial pattern, run many times — are GONE FOREVER. Rhea grieves the ones no chain reached in time. The count that was kept is dwarfed by the count that was lost; she holds both numbers and the kept one is small. Marco's indelible record (on-page chat `> marco:` CAPS/numbered/em-dash) lands the cost: the lost are now ON the record, un-erasable — named only as a count, the noun still unsaid, a column too long to scroll. The record protects what remains AND exposes the whole pattern (can't be erased). Bittersweet, real: the win is a room still full and a ledger of rooms that aren't, and both are permanent.

**5. THE SHARED BRAND — aftermath, permanent exposure.**
They are all MARKED together now (Ch25: erase one = erase all; the chain shares Russ's brand). The cost of the win: no more hiding, ever — they are known, on the record, indelibly. Render via Donna in CUES (a foreman's flat command that is really a benediction — keep it cue-short, reserve her depth for Ch27), Marco's log, the brand shared across all of them. Rhea joins it: her plain-complete disclosure is what made her the source — she is the MOST exposed and the most herself. Dry note: being un-erasable is its own kind of safety and its own kind of sentence. Theo over-talks the exposure and gets cue-cut.

**6. RUSS — resolved through company (mark stays, isolation gone).**
Russ at the door he opened — once to harm (Ch12 vise; the only way in is the door HE opened), now to keep a room full, and not alone, many hands with him. Render his eaten-word from Rhea's OUTSIDE vantage: he reaches for the cold-room noun, buckles at the article, the true noun sliding to a work-order synonym; the noun still caught at his teeth — that never changes. But the isolation is gone: the hands on the door beside his are the resolution, not any sentence he manages. "I opened it" (Ch25) was the one clean sentence; he doesn't get another — company, not exoneration. Rhea sees the mark on him is now on her too, and notes it without comfort.

**7. THE ARCHITECT THREAD — seed the finale, do NOT resolve.**
The man of total omission SLIPPED (Ch25). Render outside-only, never gloating: he is out there, no hand to grab, the inverse of the room they're standing in. Plant the finale's threat without resolving: the engine is faulted and the record is indelible, but a man who never touches anything could build it again somewhere with no door for anyone to open. The room they kept full does not reach him. The central question of Ch27 set: what do you do about a man who is never on anything — and what are they now that they're on everything. No answer here.
