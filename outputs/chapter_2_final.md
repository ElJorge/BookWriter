# Chapter 2 — The Race to Simi Valley

The word was *Delivered*, and then it wasn't.

Marco had it in his hand. Not on a nightstand this time, not flat against his sternum in the dark — in his actual fist, in the rattling vestibule between two train cars, where he'd gone because his legs would not let him sit. The 5:02 to Trenton swayed under him. Through the scratched door-glass the marsh went by in long grey smears, and Marco stared down at the thread the way you stare at a body you're not sure is breathing — at Russ's last message, at the four broken replies climbing toward it, at the dead white below — and he watched the small grey word under Russ's line.

*Delivered.*

The train took a curve. The word flickered.

*Read.*

For one heartbeat. One. Then the screen did nothing, the way screens do nothing, the cursor pulsing into a white that had no answer in it — but the word had changed. He had seen it change. Somebody on the other end of a thousand miles had put their thumb on Russ's phone and *opened it,* and Marco's own face came back at him off the dark glass, mouth open, and he was already typing before he'd decided to.

The frozen hand from an hour ago was gone. There was a task now. Tasks he could do.

> marco: STOP. Everyone stop. Russ's last message just went to Read. Someone opened it 8 seconds ago. I saw it.
> theo: WAIT
> theo: HE READ IT
> theo: he read it he read it that means he's— oh my god he's
> theo: ...wait
> theo: why isn't he typing
> theo: theo why isn't he typing

The bartender said his own name to himself, to the empty house, and there was no audience and no answer, and the hope went out of it between one line and the next.

> devang: ack. read at 1:51:42 PT. logging it.
> devang: ok. someone has device access. that's a signal. a signal with no payload is a system in an unknown state. that's worse than down.
> devang: it is not a good data point or a bad data point yet. everyone hold.
> marco: We don't hold. We move. Capitals back, here it is:
> marco: 1. Someone is physically near his phone. 2. That is not nothing. 3. We need a human being at his door — RIGHT NOW. 4. Who is the closest body to Simi Valley.

The em-dash did not come. He'd been about to number the next thing — *is he aliv* — and his thumb refused it, and Marco let the number 4 hang as a question instead. Some lists you do not finish out loud on a train.

Two thousand five hundred miles west of the marsh, the question landed in a slammed bar.

Theo had it face-up by the well, screen flaring between drink tickets, the rail a fence of them, the room a wall of sound. His hands kept making vodka sodas while the rest of him read a sentence about closest bodies. He felt the joke arrive the way he always felt it arrive — first, fast, a reflex older than thought — and he typed it before he understood what it was going to cost.

> theo: ok so geographically i'm LITERALLY the closest. weho to simi is like 45 min i'm basically already in the
> theo: i'm basically already
> theo: ...i can't leave
> theo: i have the register. i have no car today i took the bus. it's a saturday and we're four deep at the rail
> theo: i'm 45 minutes away and i can't go. i'm the closest one and i can't go

Devang read that from a nursery four hundred miles north, where a six-week-old slept on a play mat in a bar of late sun and a laptop glowed open on the changing table, and he did the only thing his nervous system had ever learned to do with a thing too big to feel. He turned it into an incident.

> devang: ok. i'm calling it. this is an incident. we work it like one or we lose the hour panicking.
> devang: we need: an IC. a comms lead. someone with eyes on—
> devang: someone with eyes on prod.
> devang: ...on russ. eyes on russ. not prod. sorry. eyes on russ.
> devang: marco you're IC. theo you're comms, you're already on the phone. i'm on data. and we need feet. we need an actual body in simi.
> theo: cole is in hermosa
> theo: cole's like an hour out but he has the truck
> marco: Cole. COLE. Are you reading this.

And under it all, where Cole would have flooded the screen — one comma-less word after another after another, the way he always did, *omg omg whats happening is he ok what do i do* — there was nothing. The most frightening thing on the screen was the place where Cole was not, and none of them clocked it. They were busy assembling a rescue, and the man they needed had already stopped answering.

> theo: cole?
> theo: cole baby answer

Then the next gut-punch, and it came from the IC.

> marco: I have a worse problem. Does anyone know his ADDRESS.
> marco: His actual street. Where his house IS.
> devang: ...
> theo: i
> theo: i don't
> theo: oh my god i don't know where russ lives

Ten years. They had his number and his brisket recipe and the entire shape of his soul, and not one of them had his street, and the silence after that question was its own small autopsy. Then Marco — Marco, who kept the history because keeping it was the closest thing he had to being kept — put his archive to work, and the thing he did instead of being held became, for ninety seconds, the thing that might save a life.

> marco: I have ten years of this thread. Stand by. I'm going in.
> marco: 12/14/19. He sent the Christmas card. There's a house number on the door behind the kid. Zooming. Hold on—
> marco: Partial. I can read three digits. Need the street.
> marco: 9/3/21 he shipped me hot sauce. The confirmation email has a redacted address but the city's there. Simi. Cross-referencing the HVAC business reg. Stand by. Stand by.

In the vestibule his thumb shook on a screen he had never once in eleven years let shake, two apps open, scrolling a decade in a minute and a half, and "stand by" came out of him twice — the archivist bleeding into the others' incident-speak, a man under more load than his own dialect could carry.

Devang worked the public records the way he worked everything, surgical, deadpan, the play mat forgotten three feet away.

> devang: i have the LLC filing. registered agent address is a PO box, useless.
> devang: pulling reviews on the business for a service-area pin. invoice metadata's a dead end, no exif.
> devang: ...ok. i have it.
> devang: address confirmed. source: a one-star yelp review that says, quote, "he never showed and the house on Camino Verde still has my deposit." one star. we have a street.

It was the funniest thing anyone had said all day and nobody laughed — a man's life cracked open by a furious customer's grudge, the group chat as forensic database, the absurdity of *that* being the door they found. Marco cross-walked the three Christmas-card digits against the street and it held. They had the house.

While they found it, Theo had been on the phone, and his actual voice was a thing none of them could hear, and it would have undone them if they could. The texts came in pieces. On the line, to the stranger, Theo was magnificent.

"Yes. I'm calling to request a welfare check," he said, and his voice was warm and level and absolutely steady, the voice of a man who has auditioned for grief commercials, the actor's armor going up over the actor's terror. "The address is twenty-two-oh-something Camino Verde, Simi Valley. The resident is — yes, I'll hold."

He held. The hold music was a tin loop of something that had once been a song. Behind him the rail screamed for drinks. And into the Chain, between the formal lines he gave the dispatcher, Theo came apart.

> theo: 911 picked up. they need a cross street. marco give me a cross street
> theo: they keep asking if he's "a danger to himself"
> theo: they keep asking that and i don't
> theo: i don't know what to say to that. what do i say. what is the answer to that

The suicide-read had just been spoken aloud by a stranger in a headset, flatly, as a checkbox, and not one of the four men could flinch-laugh past *a danger to himself.* The cursor sat there waiting for an answer none of them could type.

> marco: Say yes. Theo. Say yes so they hurry.
> theo: ...yeah. i said yes.

Then the dot moved.

Nobody had seen who dropped it. It just appeared at the bottom of the thread — a little live location card, a blue pin, and under it one text, and the text was not a word but a number, and the number was the most eloquent thing anyone had said in an hour.

> cole: 📍 live location
> cole: 52 min.

Marco stopped in the vestibule. Theo stopped pouring. Devang stared at the card on his screen and understood, all at once, what the silence had been.

> theo: cole??
> theo: cole are you DRIVING
> theo: when did you— you've been quiet for twenty minutes you've been
> theo: oh my god you're already on the road
> cole: 405. moving. don't stop texting me. i can't text back.

That was all. That was Cole — the man who never finished a hard thing, who got away on charm and luck his whole life, who had gone mute with pure terror at the end of yesterday and said *nothing.* His muteness had not been absence. It had been keys in a fist. While they hunted addresses and held for dispatchers, the silent one had walked out into the Hermosa parking lot, gotten into the truck, and started driving toward a house none of them could have found, doing the one thing he had never done — finishing the hardest thing there was — and he could not say a word about it, because words had never once been his medium for fear. Motion was. The voice-to-text came through machine-clean, punctuated by a machine for a man who never punctuated, and that cleanness was its own small horror.

The other three poured their whole terror into the moving dot.

> theo: HE'S DOING 90 IN A 65 OUR BOY CONTAINS DEPTHS
> theo: he just passed the 110 split. he's gaining. cole if you die too i will personally drive to the afterlife and—
> theo: cole if you die too i don't get to be mad at you so don't
> marco: Take the 118 west, NOT the 23. The 23 backs up at the 118 split this time of day. 118 west, Cole. I'm watching your dot.
> devang: cole's eta is holding at 48. i'm watching it. it's the only green thing on my screen right now.
> devang: status: green. i'm going to keep saying green until it isn't.

A man calling a horse race he had his whole heart bet on. An archivist aiming his precision at one blue pin's safety. An engineer monitoring a process named after his best friend. The dot crawled north and west, the clock ran, and the genre changed under them.

It was Donna.

Marco had been texting Russ's wife since yesterday, off-thread, the way you knock on a door you expect to open — and it didn't open, and now that wasn't frustrating. It was *strange.*

> marco: Donna's still not answering. I've called four times. Full voicemail or no answer, switching.
> marco: 4. I should have—
> marco: 4. Trying her sister. I have a number from a baby-shower thread in 2018.

He left the *I should have* sitting there and walked it back into a task, and what it should have said — *I'm the one who keeps everything, I should have kept where he lives, I should have known* — stayed unspoken under the number 4, which was where Marco put the things he could not afford to feel.

Theo reached the neighbor, and his voice on the line was clean and kind and steady, and the relay was not.

> theo: i got a neighbor. older guy, across the street.
> theo: he says donna's car's been gone since this morning
> theo: he thought they went somewhere together. he said "i figured they took a trip"
> theo: theo what does that mean. what does together mean

And Devang, on the data, ran his friend's life like a postmortem he was narrating to stay upright, because if he stopped narrating he would have to feel it, and feeling it would end him.

> devang: ok i have something. county records. lien filed 4 months ago against the property.
> devang: second mortgage. and the equipment loan on the business lapsed in—
> devang: the equipment loan lapsed in march.
> devang: this is what "i had it handled" was. it's money. it's a wall of money.
> devang: ok the timeline doesn't fit a—
> devang: ...the dates don't line up with a guy who just—

The man who timestamped everything could not put a date on it. The acronym died in his mouth, because finishing it would have meant naming what he was actually looking at, and the play mat was three feet away and the baby was asleep, and Devang stared at a lien filing and could not say the word.

The truck was in the driveway. The wife's car was gone. And the van — Russ's HVAC van, the one with his name on the side, the one he drove every working day of his life — nobody had thought to ask about the van. None of it added up to one clean thing. That was the new terror: not that they knew what had happened, but that they no longer did.

Then the dot crossed into Simi Valley, and the chapter ran out of air.

> devang: he's in. cole's in simi. eta 6 minutes.
> devang: 6 minutes. the welfare unit — marco where's the welfare unit
> marco: En route. ETA unknown. They WON'T give me an ETA. A unit is "en route," that's all they'll
> marco: that's all they'll say

The period was missing. The archivist who closed every thought with a period left one open, and that open end was the sound of the last man's order coming apart.

In West Hollywood, Theo set the bottle down half-poured.

The well tap ran. The glass overflowed onto the mat and kept overflowing. He untied the apron with two hands, laid it on the bar, set the register keys beside it, and walked — past the rail full of tickets, past the man calling for him, out the door into the white slap of a Saturday sidewalk where his body finally had somewhere it wanted to go and no way at all to get there. He stood on the curb with the phone in his hand and forty-five impossible minutes between him and the only thing that mattered, and he typed, and there was no GIF and no stretched vowel and no *babyyy.*

> theo: i left. i'm walking. i can't get there but i couldn't stay.

The performer had stepped off the stage for the first time in his life, and he'd done it nearly without a word, and that wordlessness coming from *him* was the most frightening thing in the thread.

> devang: cole's 2 minutes out.
> devang: i don't have a runbook for this.
> devang: there's no— there's no runbook.

And then the dot slowed, and slowed, and the numbers came one last time, and between them, after twenty miles of nothing but distance, one human sentence punched up through the machine-clean voice-to-text and gutted all of them.

> cole: 2 min.
> cole: don't make me do this alone.
> cole: here.

The dot stopped.

And the Chain — which had screamed and joked and triaged and bled for an hour, which had never once since yesterday gone quiet — went quiet, because the one man who could speak was out of the truck and there, and past speech.

It was the kind of street where the houses all agreed with each other. Cole's boots hit the driveway and the heat came up off the concrete into his face, and he stood for a second in a silence so total he could hear the tick.

Russ's truck sat in the driveway, nose-in, its engine still ticking — that slow metal *tink, tink* of a block cooling, the sound of a thing driven and then, very recently, stopped. The garage door was down. The side gate stood open, swung in on its own hinge, the way a gate stands open when somebody has gone through it and not come back to close it. And along the side of the house, in the bald rectangle of dead grass where it always lived, where the oil-stain still mapped its shape on the concrete — the HVAC van was gone.

Cole went to the kitchen window because the kitchen light was on.

Through the glass: a table set for one. One plate, one fork, one folded napkin, squared. A laptop, closed. And in the center of the table, weighted under the saltshaker so it would not move, a single sheet of paper, white, with writing on it he could not read through the glass and the heat-shimmer off the sill. The house was too neat. It was the neatness of a thing finished, or the neatness of a thing being left, and Cole could not tell which, and that not-telling was a hand around his throat. No body. No Russ. No Donna. No daughter. Just a table laid for one man and a page he wasn't meant to need a saltshaker to keep.

Behind him, in his pocket, the phone he could no longer answer was filling with the others.

> marco: Cole. Cole are you there. Say anything.
> theo: cole
> theo: cole please. one word. give me one word
> devang: no status for 90 seconds. cole. report. please just— cole.

He couldn't hear them. He was a thousand miles from the thread and one inch from the door, and his hand was already coming up toward the gate, toward the side door, toward whatever the kitchen kept — and the phone lit.

Not the Chain.

A direct message. From Russ's number. To Cole. Only to Cole.

> Russ: don't come in. i'm not there. it's not what you think. call donna NOW —

And it stopped. Mid-sentence, mid-breath, the cursor of it pulsing after the dash into nothing, the warmth stripped out and the *fellas* gone and the little raised thumb gone and only the cold left, one word shouting in caps for the first time in ten years — a man alive enough to type, alive enough to *watch,* steering the one of them who had come, the silent son, away from his own front door.

The truck ticked. The gate hung open. The page sat under the saltshaker where Cole still could not read it.

And Cole — who had finished the hardest thing he had ever started, who had driven a thousand silences north to stand in this driveway — put his hand flat on the door he had just been told not to open.
