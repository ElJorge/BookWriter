# The Chain

*A thriller-comedy in twenty-seven chapters.*

## Chapter 1 — The Last Ordinary Morning

Nobody refreshed. Nobody waited. Theo was pouring a well vodka soda for a man in sunglasses indoors, and Cole was a dot on a green wall of water, and Devang had a newborn's whole weight rising and falling on his ribs, and Marco — Marco, on a New Jersey train platform, pocketed his phone after typing a single line he didn't connect to anything:

And the chair where Russ always sat, the chair that had filled itself instantly ten thousand times, stayed empty, and not one of the four men could see it because they were sitting in it. That is the cruelty of a thing that has never once failed you. You stop being able to see the place where it lives.

The train came. Marco got on.

He read it a third time, the way you re-read a word until it stops being a word, and his thumb hovered over the keyboard he had never once in eleven years failed to fill, and nothing came out of him at all.

Behind a bar in West Hollywood Theo felt the buzz and glanced down mid-pour and the bottle kept going and the glass went over the rim and onto his hand and he didn't feel it. In Hermosa Beach Cole came up the sand toward a phone he'd left for an hour, dripping, happy, and picked it up to a screen that had changed the shape of the day. In San Francisco Devang, his daughter asleep on his chest, read the message and went so still that the baby stirred.

Four phones, four cities, one sentence, and the floor went out from under all of it at once.

And under all of it, where Cole always spiraled, where Cole always flooded the screen with one frantic comma-less word after another after another, there was nothing. The most frightening thing on the screen was the empty space where Cole would have been. He sat down in the wet sand with the phone in both hands and he did not type, because the only thing in the world he could not charm or paddle or spiral his way out of had finally arrived, and he watched the bottom of the thread for three dots that did not come.

No dots. No reply. No read receipt under Russ's last line.

The Chain, which had never gone more than minutes, went still.

A phone lay face-up — on a train seat, on a bar mat, in the sand, against a sleeping newborn's back, it did not matter which, it was the same screen in four hands — and it glowed in the failing light, and at the bottom of it Russ's last message sat alone, and stacked beneath it the four desperate replies climbed up toward him and stopped, and below the last of them the cursor blinked into a white nothing where an answer had always, always been.

The day had begun mid-laugh.

It ended in the held silence of an entire country leaning toward one dark rectangle, waiting for it to light.

It did not light.

## Chapter 2 — The Race to Simi Valley

The word was *Delivered*, and then it wasn't.

Marco had it in his hand. Not on a nightstand this time, not flat against his sternum in the dark — in his actual fist, in the rattling vestibule between two train cars, where he'd gone because his legs would not let him sit. The 5:02 to Trenton swayed under him. Through the scratched door-glass the marsh went by in long grey smears, and Marco stared down at the thread the way you stare at a body you're not sure is breathing — at Russ's last message, at the four broken replies climbing toward it, at the dead white below — and he watched the small grey word under Russ's line.

*Delivered.*

The train took a curve. The word flickered.

*Read.*

For one heartbeat. One. Then the screen did nothing, the way screens do nothing, the cursor pulsing into a white that had no answer in it — but the word had changed. He had seen it change. Somebody on the other end of a thousand miles had put their thumb on Russ's phone and *opened it,* and Marco's own face came back at him off the dark glass, mouth open, and he was already typing before he'd decided to.

The frozen hand from an hour ago was gone. There was a task now. Tasks he could do.

That was Cole — the man who never finished a hard thing, who got away on charm and luck his whole life, who had gone mute with pure terror at the end of yesterday and said *nothing.* His muteness had not been absence. It had been keys in a fist. While they hunted addresses and held for dispatchers, the silent one had walked out into the Hermosa parking lot, gotten into the truck, and started driving toward a house none of them could have found, doing the one thing he had never done — finishing the hardest thing there was — and he could not say a word about it, because words had never once been his medium for fear. It was Donna.

In West Hollywood, Theo set the bottle down half-poured.

The well tap ran. The glass overflowed onto the mat and kept overflowing. He untied the apron with two hands, laid it on the bar, set the register keys beside it, and walked — past the rail full of tickets, past the man calling for him, out the door into the white slap of a Saturday sidewalk where his body finally had somewhere it wanted to go and no way at all to get there. He stood on the curb with the phone in his hand and forty-five impossible minutes between him and the only thing that mattered, and he typed, and there was no GIF and no stretched vowel and no *babyyy.*

> theo: i left. i'm walking. i can't get there but i couldn't stay.

The performer had stepped off the stage for the first time in his life, and he'd done it nearly without a word, and that wordlessness coming from *him* was the most frightening thing in the thread.

And then the dot slowed, and slowed, and the numbers came one last time, and between them, after twenty miles of nothing but distance, one human sentence punched up through the machine-clean voice-to-text and gutted all of them.

The dot stopped.

And the Chain — which had screamed and joked and triaged and bled for an hour, which had never once since yesterday gone quiet — went quiet, because the one man who could speak was out of the truck and there, and past speech.

It was the kind of street where the houses all agreed with each other. Cole's boots hit the driveway and the heat came up off the concrete into his face, and he stood for a second in a silence so total he could hear the tick.

Russ's truck sat in the driveway, nose-in, its engine still ticking — that slow metal *tink, tink* of a block cooling, the sound of a thing driven and then, very recently, stopped. The garage door was down. The side gate stood open, swung in on its own hinge, the way a gate stands open when somebody has gone through it and not come back to close it. And along the side of the house, in the bald rectangle of dead grass where it always lived, where the oil-stain still mapped its shape on the concrete — the HVAC van was gone.

Cole went to the kitchen window because the kitchen light was on.

Through the glass: a table set for one. One plate, one fork, one folded napkin, squared. A laptop, closed. And in the center of the table, weighted under the saltshaker so it would not move, a single sheet of paper, white, with writing on it he could not read through the glass and the heat-shimmer off the sill. The house was too neat. It was the neatness of a thing finished, or the neatness of a thing being left, and Cole could not tell which, and that not-telling was a hand around his throat. No body. No Russ. No Donna. No daughter. Just a table laid for one man and a page he wasn't meant to need a saltshaker to keep.

Behind him, in his pocket, the phone he could no longer answer was filling with the others.

> marco: Cole. Cole are you there. Say anything.
> theo: cole
> theo: cole please. one word. give me one word
> devang: no status for 90 seconds. cole. report. please just— cole.

He couldn't hear them. He was a thousand miles from the thread and one inch from the door, and his hand was already coming up toward the gate, toward the side door, toward whatever the kitchen kept — and the phone lit.

Not the Chain.

A direct message. From Russ's number. To Cole. Only to Cole.

> Russ: don't come in. i'm not there. it's not what you think. call donna NOW —

And it stopped. Mid-sentence, mid-breath, the cursor of it pulsing after the dash into nothing, the warmth stripped out and the *fellas* gone and the little raised thumb gone and only the cold left, one word shouting in caps for the first time in ten years — a man alive enough to type, alive enough to *watch,* steering the one of them who had come, the silent son, away from his own front door.

## Chapter 3 — The Runbook

The door gave.

It was unlocked — that was the first thing, before the smell, before the table, before any of it. ## Chapter 4 — One of You

The light finished its turn across the wall and went out, and into the dark it left behind came a fist on the door.

I mean that. ## Chapter 7 — The Man in the Van

The body came up the outside stairs one floor too low to be the danger and one floor too high to do anything about it, and Russ watched it climb from sixty feet away with his hands at ten and two on a wheel that wasn't going anywhere.

The van idled. He'd left it running because a man who turns his engine off has decided something, and he had not decided anything, he was only sitting, the way you sit in a truck outside a job you've already quoted, waiting on a part. Lights off. Dome light off — he'd popped the little plastic cover and pulled the bulb himself an hour into this, the same way he'd pull a bulb on any fixture throwing light where light made things worse, and he'd thought, doing it, *real professional, a man stages his own death and remembers to mind the wiring,* and he'd set the bulb in the cup holder where it rolled when he took a corner, and that was the closest thing to company he had in here.

Through the windshield, up and across the dark lot, one floor up, was a door. Brass chain you couldn't see from here and a number you could — 214 — and a curtain with a bar of parking-lot light laid across it like a level somebody had set down and walked away from. Cross the lot. Take the stairs two at a time the way you used to before the knees. Open the door.*

He breathed. He set his thumb against the wheel and pressed it flat, the way you press a thing to be sure it's there.

He had meant it then about meat. He meant it now about himself, about his own foot, which he kept off the gas, about his own hand, which he kept at ten, then two, then back to ten. The wheel did nothing. He held it like it might. A man who is steering cannot also be a man who has parked his van forty feet from his family and is choosing, with both hands, to do nothing — and so Russ steered, careful, a vehicle that wasn't moving, and watched the body climb, and that was the bottom of the well, and that was where it found him: narrating the well, because narrating it small was the only thing his hands had ever known how to do, and the only audience left was a dashboard, and the dashboard was the worst he'd ever had, and it was the only one who couldn't get up and leave.
