Fourteen phones, fanned across the long table like a hand she'd dealt herself, and Donna was folding it one card at a time.

She thumbed the back off the first, popped the battery, set the dead thing in a row at the table's edge. Thirteen. The cover came off the next with the same small dry click, a sound like a knuckle, and she lined the corpse up beside its brother, square to the edge, the way you lay out anything you've finished with and want to be able to count later. Twelve. Her hands knew this. Her hands had been doing some version of this for thirty hours and would not be told the job was done.

It was done. That was the part her body wouldn't take. The rescue was over and her family was upstairs, asleep in beds she could walk to, and she was down here in the back room behind Theo's bar pulling batteries out of burners because sitting felt like falling and she had not yet figured out how to land.

Eleven. Ten.

"Fourteen funerals," Theo said from the bar end, stacking glasses that did not need stacking, "and not one casserole. You'd think somebody'd —" He set a glass down. Picked up another. The rest of the sentence had a hole in it where the funny used to live, and he reached into the hole, found nothing, and let his hand come back empty. He stacked the glass. The riff just stopped, the way a car stops when the road does.

Donna didn't look at him. Looking at Theo too hard right now was a thing you did to a man you wanted to break. She gave him the dignity of the teardown instead. Nine.

Across the room the corkboards were coming down in strips, and the network was going back into its box one node at a time — Devang somewhere remote, reabsorbing the rig, his cleanup scrolling up a screen propped against a beer crate.

> devang: decommissioning the relay cluster. fourteen handsets going dark in my logs too. very tidy. like watching a city lose power one window at a time, except i'm the one flipping the breakers and i feel WEIRD about it, thanks for asking

Eight. Seven. Marco was already archiving the thing while it was still warm, while the rest of them were still bleeding from it, his entries clicking up the screen in capitals, filing the rescue into the record like a man pressing a flower that hadn't finished dying.

Six. Five.

Her hand came down the row and passed over the one phone she had not killed.

She did it on the next pass too. Passed over it. The fourteenth card. Still lit, still warm, the only live thing in a dead hand — the one Cole had shoved into Russ's chest at the end, the one holding the name. She knew what was on it. That was the whole trouble. She had not asked for it and she could not put it down, and a foreman who'd just gotten everyone home was standing here at one in the morning learning that someone had already loaded the next weight onto her back before she'd set the first one down.

The win, she thought, has a hole in it the same shape as the hole in Theo's joke.

Four. Three. She re-sealed it, the way she sealed everything, and went back to the count.

---

She killed the rest. Then she picked up the live one and woke it.

RHEA OKONKWO-DALE.

The name sat there on the screen, complete, with coordinates under it and a credential string under that, and Donna waited for it to do what those names did. When the crew had to speak about the others — the ones from before, the room that was full and then wasn't — the noun went soft in the mouth and died on the way out, every time, a word that refused to be a word. She braced her jaw for that. She set herself for the buckle.

It didn't come.

"Rhea Okonkwo-Dale," she said. Flat. A foreman checking a weld, tapping it once to hear if it rang true.

It rang. It held. She said it again, quieter, because it had held — and it held again, easy, no give in it anywhere. That was the wrong answer. Everything the collector touched had a give in it somewhere; that was how you found the man, by the place the thing flexed. A clean name with no flex was a chain with no links, and she built chains for a living, so she knew exactly what an unlinked person was. She set the phone down and it stayed lit, accusing her of something she hadn't done yet.

Cole was across the table, still as a hung coat, watching her say it. He'd heard the name not buckle too. He let out one slow breath through his nose and tapped two fingers once against the wood. That was all. The wordless one always heard the un-eaten word loudest.

The screen flickered as Marco caught the entry and chased it.

> marco: LOGGED — RHEA OKONKWO-DALE — AND THEN NOTHING. NO ALIASES. NO PRIORS. NO CO-SIGNERS. NO ENTANGLEMENTS. I PUT MY WHOLE WEIGHT ON THIS NAME AND — THE RECORD WON'T HOLD ONTO HER. NOTHING TO GRAB.

"Most people," came Devang, dry, "you'd kill to find this little on. Her, it's the problem."

---

"Contacts," Donna said.

> devang: corporate hotline. that's it.

"Next of kin."

> devang: same hotline. she's listed it as her own ICE contact. emergency contact set to herself.

Theo made a sound that was almost a laugh and didn't reach being one. "Can't build a chain," he said, "out of a woman who keeps her own next-of-kin set to herself." Dark, true — and he let it lie there because there was nowhere kind to take it.

"Anybody," Donna said. Her questions were getting shorter. The shrinking question was the shrinking hope. She'd come into this running the protocol she knew — find the chain, reinforce the chain, that was the job, everyone had a chain — and the protocol kept returning null. You could not reinforce a chain that was not there.

> marco: SHE MOVES EVERY 18 MONTHS. NO LEASE OVERLAP. NO STANDING DINNER. NO GROUP THREAD. NO ONE WHO'D FILE A REPORT INSIDE A WEEK. THIS ISN'T NEGLECT — IT'S 1) DELIBERATE 2) MAINTAINED 3) GOOD AT IT. SHE BUILT THIS ON PURPOSE.

Donna had been leaning over the screen. Now she straightened, because there was nothing left to lean toward.

The channel went quiet, and then Devang came back, and when he came back the systems had fallen off his voice.

> devang: there's no...

> devang: there's no upstream. nobody's pinging her. i keep looking for the node she connects to and there isn't one. nobody'd know the packet dropped.

It sat in the room. Even Theo's hands went still on the glasses.

That was the shape of it, then, laid bare on the table next to the dead phones. The collector had not found a weak link. There were no links to find weak. He had found a person built, carefully, on purpose, to have no many — and a job that needed exactly one signature no one would ever stand behind to witness. Independence, the thing you praised in a woman, the thing Rhea had clearly chosen and tended like a garden. That was the selectability. The very thing that should have kept her safe was the thing that put her name on the card.

---

And Donna understood, standing there, that the card had her name on it too.

Because the man had learned to model a many — they'd proven that, last time, the hard way — and now he'd reached past the many and picked the one target her gift couldn't simply wrap. She read flawed hands true and she built chains around the people the world had left loose. That was the difference between her and the men who reached into lives uninvited: she offered. She read a hand and she offered, she didn't take. There was always someone to ratify it, someone whose presence turned her reach from a theft into a gift.

Rhea had no one to ratify anything.

Which meant, from the outside, wrapping a chain around a woman who had spent her whole life refusing one would look like exactly what the collector did. A hand reaching into a stranger's life. Deciding for her. Arranging her without her consent. There was no witness here to make Donna's reach a gift instead of the other thing, and the collector knew it, and that was the point — he hadn't found the weakness in the crew. He'd found the weakness inside her virtue and built the whole job around it.

She looked at the live phone the way you look at a door you swore you'd never open.

"We knock," she said. "We don't pick the lock. She gets a vote."

Three beats, command not confession, and her hand was already moving to the next thing before anyone could turn it into a conversation. Armor. They took it as a cue, because that was what it was shaped like, and went back to work hearing an order where the question lived underneath. Theo's mouth opened. "And if she says —" The thread ran out of road right where the unbearable part started, and he let it drop, and stacked a glass.

Donna didn't answer it. She couldn't. The clock was the collector's, not hers, and a vote on a timeline she controlled was not a vote, and she knew that, and she set her jaw and did not say it, and could not quite make her hand let go of the phone.

---

"Russ," she said.

She'd run the hand already, in her head, the way she ran the burners — every card and where it could go. Not Theo; he'd frozen once, and the wound was a day and a half old, and you did not test that on a stranger's life. Not Cole; you could not send silence to do a trust-talk. Not her; she'd be the uninvited hand she'd just sworn off, walking up to a fortress to decide its fate in person.

Russ. The only card that fit. The only card that was also, by a long way, the most likely to lose.

Because the careful solo woman would vet the stranger at her counter — she was careful, that was the whole problem — and the instant she did she'd find the record, and the record named Russ now, plain and searchable, the man who opened the door, the exoneration gone. The brand would reach the counter before he did. Perfect emotional fit, worst possible credential, and the cruelty was that the fit and the credential were the same thing: he was the only one who understood her selectability because he'd been a key, spent by a hand that reached into his life uninvited, and that was precisely why the world would never let him near her clean.

He came off the wall where he'd been standing, branded and quiet, a tool he hadn't asked to be again.

"I'll go," Russ said. Then, lower, trying to say why he understood — "she got picked the way they got picked, the ones from the —" and the noun went out from under him, the word for the room dying in his throat the way it always died, swallowed before it could be a thing. He didn't finish it. He never could.

Donna picked up the live phone. She held it out, and his hand came to it, and for a beat too long both their hands were on the same warm card, and the dock passed through her — the night she'd reached down and pulled this man off a different edge, years gone, a thing she did not say and would not say and let move through her and out the other side. He was going to do for a stranger the thing she'd done for him. She let go first.

---

Before he left they tried, once, to do the old thing.

> devang: ok. who scheduled the dark window. who signed the manifest first. give me the originating call and i'll find his hand on it.

The answers came back and not one of them had an owner.

> devang: there's no originating call. the supplier produced this window, the scheduler produced this window, the depot's own system produced this window — INDEPENDENTLY. all three. no source packet. i can't find where it starts. it's like... it arranged itself.

> marco: LAST TIME WE WERE THE MANY AND HE WAS THE ONE AND WE WON BY BEING UNPARSEABLE. THIS TIME HE'S THE MANY-THROUGH-ONE AND I CAN'T — THERE IS NO ONE TO POINT AT. NO SEAM. NO HAND. IT READS LIKE WEATHER.

Donna stood very still. The foreman who could always find the next thing to carry, with nothing in reach to pick up. Last time they'd beaten a single patient hand by being a crowd it couldn't read. Now the crowd was his, routed clean through one channel with no findable one inside it, and her whole trade was grabbing the link that flexed, and there was no link. The signature weapon — be a many, find the one — was just dead on the table next to the burners.

"So we bring the old play," Russ said, out of muscle memory, already knowing.

> devang: man. last time i told you there's no API for a man standing in a parking lot. there's no API for a locked door.

> devang: this time there's no door at all. he took the door.

It deflated something, the way it was meant to, and the deflation came out the bottom as resolve, because that was the only place left for it to go.

---

Russ pocketed the phone. Cole crossed to him.

The room leaned in. Donna felt it lean, felt all of them tilt toward the silent man at the center as if this — the send-off, the live card going out the door — might finally be the moment he'd open his mouth and the bank would break and they'd hear it, the first word, the name or the warning or whatever he'd been holding all this time.

Cole drew a breath. It caught at the top, loaded, a word right there at the edge of it, the whole room hanging on the catch —

— and he closed his mouth. He pressed the phone deeper into Russ's hand, both his hands around Russ's one, and held his eye, and that was the word. *Not now. Not this.* He'd spend it on something that earned it, and a man going out a door to find a stranger was not that, and he knew it the way he knew everything, wordlessly, and chose. The bank got heavier. Russ took the weight of the silence as the blessing it was and nodded.

"Go," Donna said at his back. One cue. "Knock."

He went out the half-inch the back door stood open, into the night, toward a woman who answered her own phone. And Donna watched the door — and did not close it. It hung there, a single live opening in a room she'd spent the night shutting down, the last card walked out of the folded hand, and she found she could not quite get her arm to swing it to.

---

The phone in her ear came alive forty minutes later. Fluorescent hum under it, the clean dead air of a counter at three a.m. off the 5. Russ's footsteps. Then a voice, even, unhurried, every clause finishing exactly where it meant to.

"You're the man who opened the door."

No fear in it. Donna's hand went flat on the table. This was not a woman waiting to be saved; this was a fortress with all its lights on.

"I read it before you parked," Rhea said. "I read everything. I don't have anybody to call about it — so I called no one. I want you to account for yourself, completely, before I give you a single thing." A pause, level as a shelf. "What do you want."

Clean voice, Donna thought, the read landing cold and whole. No gaps. Nothing held back. She protects no one, so she hides nothing — and that's it, that's the wound, that's why she could be modeled down to the manifest: she's legible. She's already voted. She's voted by knowing everything, and there's no reach left that I could disguise as an offer, because she sees the hand coming. *She gets a vote* — she already cast it.

And under the voice, on the second channel, Donna saw it begin to move — the dark window, sliding earlier, the seamless many feeling the approach and tightening with no source she could name, two clocks now running at once.

Across the line she heard Russ open his mouth — the man who was best at saying nothing about to try, against everything in him, to *say*, to earn with words a trust the record swore he had no right to.

"Ms. Dale," he said, "I'm not here to —"
