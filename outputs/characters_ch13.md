## RULINGS ON THE TWO FLAGGED QUESTIONS

### Ruling 1 — Which mid-word cut is the load-bearing hook? → **THE COLLECTOR.** Cole stays wordless; do NOT spend his first word here.
Primary hook = **the collector cut mid-word: "It seems you've all decided to —".** This is the load-bearing close. Reasoning:
- **It is the thesis, dramatized.** The whole book argues a MANY cannot be handled by a man who models INDIVIDUALS. The most devastating proof is not a hero's triumphant line — it is the *handler himself* losing the ability to finish a sentence. The device that ate Russ ("Russ was the —" in Ch12) now eats the COLLECTOR. The eraser turns on the man who weaponized it. That is structural justice the reader feels in the body.
- **It escalates, doesn't repeat (the plot_architect's stated requirement).** Ch12 snatched a NOUN ("the —"). Ch13 snatches a RESULT/verdict from the one character who has never lacked the next move. The frictionless man with nothing to grip is finally underspecified — his model returned null and his MOUTH returns null with it. Same signature, raised one floor.
- **Cole's first word is a one-time detonator — too valuable to spend muffled.** If we burn Cole's first-ever word by having it eaten before it forms, we get a smaller version of a device we already used on Russ, AND we permanently lose the bigger payoff (Cole's first word landing INTACT in a later chapter). Ruling: Cole stays fully wordless through Ch13. Keep the powder dry.
- **Layering (allowed, subordinate):** You MAY let Russ's status flicker toward resolving on the channel a half-beat before the collector's line ("russ is —") so the reader's hope spikes — but the *snatched* word, the one the chapter dies on, is the collector's. Donna's POV is on her hand at the half-unlocked door; the collector's unfinished line is the last thing she hears. Land the collector's cut as the final text on the page.

### Ruling 2 — How hard to surface the cold-room noun (PEOPLE)? → **NEAR-NAME via grammatical failure; never print the noun. Threshold = one degree from the word, blocked by the eraser.**
Precise threshold for Beat 8:
- Russ's synonym chain runs "rows / units / product." At the cold room opening, "rows" **buckles** — Russ is looking right at it, so the synonym can no longer hold, and the sentence reaches for the true noun and *fails at the article*: e.g., the construction gets as far as **"these aren't units, they're —"** and the eraser takes it. The reader's mind completes "people" in the white space; the page does not.
- **Do not** let any other character say it either (no helpful Marco caption naming it). The team understands via the GAP, not via a printed word — that is the entire mechanic. If anyone "says" it, it is in a non-word: a breath, a stop, Donna's inventory going silent for one line.
- The recontextualization (schedule = ERASURE not delivery; the two opposite acts collapse into one) is allowed to be UNDERSTOOD fully and stated structurally ("completion" was the disappearance of the thing in the room) — but the noun for *what disappears* stays off-page. Series keeps one turn of the screw.
- One controlled exception to "never on page": the collector at Beat 9 may brush the truth in pure logistics language ("the count," "the release") — euphemism, never the noun — which makes his courtesy more obscene, not less. That sharpens the horror without spending the reveal.
