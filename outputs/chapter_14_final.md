The green light came up under his knuckles, and his hand wasn't shaking.

That was the problem. Theo held Russ's badge flat to the reader, the little laminated rectangle still warm from his own grip, and the pad woke green — a soft, agreeable, lawnmower-on-a-Sunday green — and his hand lay across it like something that belonged to a calmer man. He looked at it. He kept looking at it, the way you look at a phone you're sure is about to ring.

*Okay. Okay, so — that's good, isn't it, green is good, green is yes, green is open-sesame, green is the absence of a man in a vest asking you to step over here, sir,* he told the inside of his own skull, which was the only audience he had, *and the hand is steady, which, which is — no, see, normally the hand does a thing, normally there's a tremor, normally I get to watch the tremor and think, ah, good, that's the part of me that knows we're in trouble, that's the smoke detector, that's the —*

The hand did nothing. The hand was a brick. The smoke detector had been removed and pocketed by someone, and Theo did not trust anyone who'd been in his body lately enough to know who.

In his ear: the silence. And inside the silence, still hanging, the sentence the collector had started and — for the first time in everything, in the whole long polite siege of him — *not finished:*

*"It seems you've all decided to —"*

To. To what. Theo's whole life was finishing other people's sentences, snatching the word out of the air before they could fumble it, *to disappear, to fold, to — no, don't,* and his mouth ached to grab the end of this one and run, and he clamped down on it because completing that sentence felt exactly like reaching into a garbage disposal to see if it was off.

And under it, threaded thinner, the other one. Russ's. From three minutes or three hours ago, the channel had given him *"russ is —"* and then given him nothing, a half-second of breath and then the flat clean nothing of a man who'd stopped transmitting, and Theo had decided, with the entire executive function of a startled rabbit, that he would simply not learn the rest. Russ is. Present tense. Russ *is*. As long as the verb had nowhere to land, Russ was alive in the gap, and Theo would keep him there, thanks, he'd hold the door of that little grammar open with his foot forever if he had to.

He did not know if he was standing at a door or a grave. The sodium light hummed. The HVAC had gone valve-quiet, the building breathing low and slow the way a thing breathes when it thinks the company has gone home.

---

He was clear. That was the thing he couldn't make sit down.

He'd done it — the readers, all of them, the whole bank, he'd walked Russ's credential down the line and they'd all gone green and stayed green, and the building had read the schedule and read the room and decided, in its dim institutional way, that the night's work was *complete*. Which was why the channel hadn't coughed up the back half of *russ is —*. Nobody'd withheld it. The system simply thought there was nothing left to say.

"Clear" was the word. *Clear,* he thought, standing at the open mouth of the room that had been full and then wasn't, *clear like a sky, clear like a tab, clear like a throat, none of which, when you really sat with them, were good things to be at the threshold of a room that —*

He keyed the mic. Keyed it too hard.

"Hey — hi — somebody — okay, I need a number, I need a status, I need anybody to say literally any number at me, because I'm clear, I'm green, I did the — I did all the green, and the channel's gone quiet and Russ is, Russ was, the last thing I got was Russ is and then nothing, and I would really love, I would deeply love, to know what the verb is, so if somebody could just —"

> marco: 1. READER BANK READS CLEAR.
> marco: 2. SCHEDULE READS — DONE.
> marco: 3. DON'T — DON'T KEY IT AGAIN, YOU'LL —

"I'm gonna key it again," Theo said, keying it again. "I'm sorry, I am, I know, I can read the all-caps, the all-caps is coming through, but you gave me two numbers and a dash, Marco, you gave me a *dash*, and I have had it up to here with dashes tonight —"

> devang: theo.

Just his name. Lowercase, no acronym, no systems-frame, no *the API for this is*, none of his usual scaffolding. Devang, who narrated the world in interfaces, had set all the interfaces down.

> devang: the part everyone was scared you'd lock up on. you just did it. you didn't.

Theo's mouth opened and — astonishingly, briefly — nothing came out of it.

"Yeah," he said. Small. "Yeah, okay. Thanks. Don't — say it again though, I'll cry, and crying's loud."

Then the channel came back, and it was the wrong one.

*"It seems —"* the collector said, and Theo's spine went to ice-water, because the collector had never said anything twice, the collector said a thing once, frictionless, smooth as a closed valve, calm the way a closed valve is calm — *"it seems you have — that you've — you have decided —"*

There.

There it was, and Theo had no word for it, and not having a word for things was not a state Theo's head had ever been left in before. Under the collector's voice, where for *all of it* there had been nothing, no rasp, no give, just a surface, just smooth dark glass — there was now, for one half of one beat, the ghost of an *intake.* A breath that wasn't a breath. A seam in the glass. The sound a thing might make if it had never had lungs and had just now decided it might need to find out.

"That's — no, that's not, he doesn't —" Theo whispered, reaching for the caption, his life-long reflex coming up empty for the first time, "there's no — that's not *breathing,* he doesn't *breathe,* that's the whole — okay so the scary closed door has a *pulse* now, cool, great, love that for —" and the sentence died in his mouth the way the collector's had, and Theo went still against the reader bank, still the way the rabbit goes still, the steady hand from before now steady because prey is steady.

*"You,"* the collector said, courteous as ever, the politeness not cracking, only the grammar — *"you've. You — how many hands closed this door. You have all — you. How many of you."*

Not afraid — he had no breath to be afraid with — just a valve being asked a question it was never plumbed for, recalculating, and recalculation in a frictionless thing was the worst sound Theo had ever stood inside.

He's not leaving, Theo understood. He's *learning.*

What Cole carried — the weight of it, even from here, even through a channel, Theo could feel it, the thing Cole had walked out of the cold room cradled against his chest like it might wake, the thing none of them said the name of. Cole, who didn't speak. Cole, the most exposed body on the whole board, standing in the brightening light unable to call for help because help wasn't a thing his mouth did. A tap came down the channel — Cole's mic, once, soft. A breath. That was all Cole ever gave you, and it was enough to break your heart on.

Then Donna, flat and unhurried, conducting:

"Theo. The card."

That was it. That was the whole cue. No exposition, because Donna didn't spend words she could make you find yourself, and Theo found it, standing up like a cold thing climbing his back —

*it's me. The fix is me. The fix is a — oh no. Oh no, it's a* talking *thing —*

"— surely Marco, no, the record's his, no, he just said don't key it — surely Devang, no, there's no, what'd he say, there's no API for a — okay, okay, so it's me, it's a me thing, it's a *mouth* thing, of course it's a mouth thing, it's always the mouth, the mouth is the trouble, the mouth has *always* been the —" and he became aware, in his fist, of Russ's badge, which he'd been holding this whole time like a memento, and was now, suddenly, holding like a tool.

"Breathe," Donna said.

He breathed.

---

The collector was still in his ear, still reaching, *"how many of you, how many — you've all —"* groping at the plural, the door he couldn't model, and the schedule read DONE only as long as the building believed one hand had closed one door, and the readers would relight in — Devang's number was small now, the number was very small —

Theo opened his mouth under the highest stakes he had ever held, and the thing he was certain he could not do, he did not do.

He didn't freeze.

He said, into the channel, clean, in the collector's own register, in the smooth courteous euphemism the spoof was built on: "Yes. It seems you've all decided to finish the job."

And then nothing. No parenthetical. No sorry. No narration crowding in behind the words to apologize for them. He didn't tell himself what he was doing. He just did it and let the silence stand where the caption would have gone, and the silence was the loudest thing he had ever not said.

*"— to finish the job,"* the collector repeated, and the breath-seam closed. The valve had been handed exactly the shape it could hold: one actor, one hand, one door, closing, done. The recalculation stopped.

The schedule held. The bank cycled. And in the relit corridor, Cole's hands moved — grip and lift and a body crossing on another body's strength, a tap to confirm, a held breath let out — and the readers came up clean with no one printed on them, the lit stack washing empty, Cole gone back into the dark he was never supposed to leave and a second shape coming with him on his arm.

A man. On his feet. Breathing. Real.

"Out," Donna said.

Russ.

---

He stood in front of Theo and he didn't get to be clean about it, neither of them did, because the same record that read DONE — the record that read the job *finished* and on that basis unlatched the schedule that had been holding Russ like a credential mid-spend, the record that gave him back his legs and the corridor and the next forty years — that record now said, and would say forever, in a field that did not have an undo, that the door had been opened by Russ. The access job closed in his name. He was free and he was branded, and rescue and clearing your name had turned out to be two different doors, and they'd only had the keys to one.

Russ knew it. Theo could see him doing the math on his own face.

"They had us calling them rows," Russ said. His voice came out the way a roll-down gate comes down. "On the work order. Rows. Units. *Product.* Like —" His jaw worked. "These aren't units, they're —"

And the sentence walked to the edge of the article and could not step off it. *They're.* The true word stood there on the cliff and Russ's own mouth refused to push it. The man who'd handled everyone his whole life, who could talk a guard out of a flashlight and a marshal out of a hunch, stood there and could not finish the one sentence that mattered about the room that was full and then wasn't.

And Theo — who finished everyone's sentences, who'd been finishing them since before he could spell, whose whole malfunction was that he couldn't leave a gap alone —

didn't.

He let it hang. He held Russ's eyes and he did not look away from the unsaid thing, and he did not reach in and grab the word and run it home, because for once the kindest thing his mouth could do was stay shut and let two men who couldn't finish their sentences stand together inside the same one.

At the edge, Cole's hand came to rest on Russ's shoulder. Said nothing. Said everything a hand can say.

---

The dark window closed.

Behind them, the reader Theo had been holding the badge against this whole time gave a small click and cycled, and the green he'd opened on — the lawnmower-Sunday green, the omen-green — went red. A flat, sealed, finished red, the building locking the night it thought was over.

Theo looked down at his hand.

Now it was shaking.

Now, after, with the door shut and the man breathing in front of him, the tremor arrived — the smoke detector reinstalled too late to do anything but tell the truth — and it was, for the first time in his entire narrated life, not a performance, not an alibi, not a bit. Just his hand. Just the honest fear, showing up at the door after the party, holding the card, red light pooling in the shake of it.

He opened his mouth. The reflex came back, softer now, lower. He almost made it a joke. He almost apologized to the badge.

"There it is," he said instead, quiet, to nobody, to his own hand. And let it be.

Then the channel came back one last time, and all of them heard it — Theo, and Cole's breath gone still, and Donna not keying a word, and the whole crew strung along the same dark wire — heard it together:

*"You,"* the collector said.

And it was steady.

No seam. No intake. The glass had been re-ground, the valve re-plumbed, and the breath that had almost been there was gone again, gone smoother than before, because now the surface held the plural without any strain at all.

*"You. All of you. Held as one — I could not see the shape of you, before. I see it now. It's a beautiful piece of architecture, what you are. A door no single hand has to close. I had been counting hands."* The courtesy had not cracked once. *"I think what you are, properly named, the thing I finally understand I have been looking at all along, is a —"*

And the channel cut.

In the gap where the word should have landed, where the collector had finally learned the grammar to finish it, something else came up instead — a new line surfacing clean on the dark, a string of coordinates that wasn't this building, a credential handle that wasn't Russ's, a single name none of them had ever heard, blinking once and waiting —

and Theo's own mouth started to shape a word, the smallest reflex, half a syllable of *wait* or *who* or *don't*, and swallowed it, and the line held, and the name sat there, and the sentence the collector had finally learned to finish hung snatched in the air, ending where every sentence in the worst nights of all their lives ended, on the —
