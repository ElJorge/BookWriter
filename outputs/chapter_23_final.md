Seven.

Donna counted them the way she counted burners on a line — left to right, no skipping, the number landing in her chest before it landed in her head. Cole by the door, still. Russ at the end of the table with his hands flat. Marco's face washed blue by the channel. Devang a held breath at the terminal. Theo with a roll of tape, finding somebody's loose end. Rhea, the record open in front of her like a thing she wouldn't cover.

Seven. Right. Whole.

A count came back true whether you wanted it or not.

The terminal threw its last word at the room and let it sit there, no gloat in it, just weather:

> APPROVED. CONDITIONS APPLY.

"Read me the conditions," Donna said. Cue, not a question. The room had gone quiet and scared, and when a room went quiet and scared she did the only thing that ever held it — she ran the pass. Mise en place. Who's covered. Who's got what. A heist of the truth, plated like a Friday dinner rush, and a clock that had already jumped twice eating the back half of the evening.

Devang didn't look up from the rig. "In person. On site. Inside the window." A beat, flat as a level. "And it can't be split."

"Say that last part again."

"The submission is atomic." He said it like physics, because to him it was. "One package, one entry. The system accepts a valid input. It will not accept half of one."

Donna tapped the table. Once per condition, the way she counted covers. One slot. One hand. In the window. No splitting. She did the arithmetic the only place she trusted it — out loud, in cues. "So we built the most over-engineered, can't-be-erased, hundred-handed thing anybody's ever made—" she let the absurdity stand a second, because somebody had to "—and it still needs feet."

Theo snorted, soft. "It always needs feet."

"It always needs feet," she agreed, and the joke did the small ugly work jokes did, which was hold the room up one more minute.

"Witness is sealed." Marco's voice came out of him in his own font, even aloud — like he was reading himself off the screen. "Distributed. Mirrored. Numbered. Item 9: SOURCE-RECORD ATTACHED — SIGNED — COMPLETE." He sealed the channel as he said it, a small definite click, locking a door he'd built so no one could ever quietly unbuild it.

"Record stands," Rhea said. Plain. Whole. No flourish on it, because the thing didn't need one. "Every name. Mine on it." She turned the page a degree so the line with her name didn't hide. Her name sat there clean and finished, the way a name should, and under it the long fall of the others, the many of them, each one a person who'd said *put me down too.* Erase one, erase all. Not a file. A column.

Donna watched it plate up. Devang's leg, Marco's leg, Rhea's leg — three stations landing at once, the pass coming together the way a line did on the good nights, every cover up at the same second, nothing dying under the heat lamp. The gun was loaded. She felt the grim small satisfaction of a thing well built, and right behind it, riding its tail, the other thing.

Because a loaded gun had to be walked into the room.

Her eye went to Cole's hands. It kept doing that. Courier's hands, folded and still by the door — hands that had carried other people's words across this city for years and never once put a word of their own down on top of them. Still hands. The stillest in the room.

"Column can't go in pieces," she said, to the room, to herself. "One body takes the whole thing in. One body."

Nobody said the name. They didn't have to. The role had already said it; she was only the last to catch up.

She didn't look at his face. Faces lied to her all day. She looked at his hands, because hands told her the truth even when their owner was trying to keep it — and Cole's hands were already saying yes. Not slumped. Not given-up. *Ready.* The particular readiness of a man who has decided a thing and is only waiting for someone to make it official.

"You carry," Donna said. Cue. A name without the name. A foreman setting a station.

Cole's jaw set and held.

That was all, at first — the jaw. Then a breath went into him and stayed, gathering somewhere behind his teeth, building toward something with weight and shape, and Donna watched it climb and watched it not come out. A knuckle whitened on the table's edge. There was a thing at the top of him, loaded, pressed up under the lid, and she could *feel* it the way you feel a pot a half-second before it goes over — and it didn't go over. It held. He held it.

Then he did the thing that took the floor out from under her: he reached, and he took the column off the table, and he squared it against his chest the way he'd squared ten thousand packages — a reflex, a courier's tidy automatic *received* — like it was a parcel and not a list of everyone, like it weighed what paper weighs.

He took everyone's words like any other delivery. The one man in the room with none of his own.

Donna swallowed something and counted instead.

"Approach," she said. "Walk me the approach."

This was where the wall came up. She watched it come up in Russ.

"Submission point's inside," Marco said. "Behind the door. Only one door takes the key, and the key's branded to one—"

"I know whose," Donna said, quiet, because Marco didn't need to finish it and Russ didn't need to hear it built brick by brick.

She looked at Russ the way she looked at hands. She'd pulled him off a dock once — wet, half-gone, heavier than a man should be — and she knew the architecture of his face the way she knew the line. So she saw it happen. She saw the true word start up in him, the real one, the noun for the place and the people who'd been in it — and she saw it go *down.* The jaw worked once. A swallow. The buckle caught somewhere around *the*, around the article, the little hinge a true sentence turns on, and what surfaced on the far side came up sanded clean.

"The," Russ said. "—the site." His hand near the table went flat. "I have to be on site. To open the—" the catch again, the noun ducking under "—the entry. Nobody else's key turns it."

*The room that was full and then wasn't.* He couldn't say it and she wouldn't make him. He'd opened that door once to let harm in. Now he had to stand at it again and open it to let the truth through — the same hand, the same brand, the exact opposite errand, and Donna felt the shape of it without saying its name, the same hand at the same kind of door, sent the other way.

Two of them, then. The one who carries everything and opens nothing. The one who opens the one door and carries the brand. She saw how the two of them landed where the work needed them, and refused to admire it.

"Clock," she said.

"Window's tightening," Devang said, and the terminal jumped under him, the number dropping where the room could see it:

> DARK WINDOW: 00:41 — 00:39

"Forty," Donna said. "Live check. Radio."

"Two." Theo held one up, thumbed it, got the small honest crackle. "Both warm. Spare batteries in the—" he patted Cole's jacket pocket, "—there."

"Watch."

"Real one." Theo unstrapped his own off his wrist and pressed it into Cole's palm, closing the courier's fingers over it with both of his hands, a second longer than tying off a loose end took. "Don't trust your phone. Phones lie about the time."

"Phones lie about everything," Theo added, and there it was — the too-human joke, landing soft, not a flood now but a current, the over-talker grown into the one who kept the room's hands moving so nobody had to feel the minute. It landed. A couple of breaths in the room came easier.

"Who's on the rig," Donna said.

"Me." Marco. "All three of you on channel. Manifest's locked. Item 1: CARRIER ON SITE. Item 2: KEY ON SITE. Item 3: VALID INPUT SUBMITTED — and item 4 trips on its own." He didn't let item 4 hang; he closed it. "Item 4: WITNESS FIRES. Already armed."

"Even if—" Donna started, and stopped, because she didn't want the rest of it in the air.

Devang gave her the rest of it anyway, because that was the mercy he had to give, the only one. "The witness fires regardless." Flat. Cold all the way down, and she understood that the cold was how he carried it, that warm would have cracked the spec. "Independent of carrier status. If the two of them don't walk back out that door, the record still goes. The thing works whether or not they—" he didn't soften it, "—it works."

So they were provisioning two things at one table. A heist of the truth and, in the same breath, with the same tape and the same spare batteries, a possible funeral. Both registers, all the way down to the watch. Donna let neither of them win.

"Lock-up," she said.

"I lock up," Rhea said. Plain. Whole. "When you're gone I lock the door behind you and I stay on the record." She didn't dress it. "Thank you for—" a small pause, no flourish, "—for letting me be held. Whatever it costs."

Forty minutes ago there'd been an abstract weapon in this room. Now there were two coats being zipped.

And here was the part the foreman gets, the only part, the one she'd known was coming since the count came back whole: she had to *send* them.

She'd built chains her whole life. She knew exactly what a chain was for and exactly what she was — the one who held it. Holding a chain was one thing. Feeding it out a door was another, and she stood in the gap now and it cost her in a place she kept locked.

Her hand came up. It meant to reach — for Cole's shoulder, for Russ's arm, for the dock-pull muscle that had hauled a drowning man up out of cold water years ago, the muscle that knew how to bring a person *in.* She felt it lift with that intent, the old reflex, *grab and hold, don't let go* —

— and she turned it. Mid-air, she turned the hand over and opened it and let it become a point. Toward the door. The opposite work. Pulling a man in had never asked anything of her she wasn't glad to give. Opening her hand to send two out asked everything, and she paid it in that half-second, and her face did one thing it was not supposed to do, one small flinch under the dry steel —

— and then she sealed it, the way you re-seal a split in a line you can't stop to fix, folding the crack right into the order so the order carried it out.

"You chose this," she said. Not soft. True. They had — the vote, every hand up. She wasn't spending them. She'd read every hand in this room true and not one of them had said *no,* and she would not insult the *yes* by trying to take it back now. The terminal upstairs could approve a man and never see him, could spend a body like a number and feel nothing leave. She would not name it, because naming it would make it a speech and they didn't have time for a speech, and a speech would have been *her* comfort, not theirs.

"So I'm sending you," she said. "Not throwing you. Sending you. There's a difference and you'll feel it the whole way." Her open hand stayed open, aimed at the door. "Go well."

Russ nodded, the flat acknowledgment of a man who would not be soothed. Cole's eyes came up off the column to her face — just once, just a beat — and the thing in him pressed higher, right to the lid, that gathered breath at the very top of his throat where a word would live if he had one to spend, loaded to the absolute edge, *right there* —

— and it didn't fire. He banked it. He gave her the nod and the breath stayed in.

"Move," Donna said.

They moved.

And the count went the other way.

She did it without deciding to — the reflex, one last time, eyes left to right across the room as the two coats crossed it. Marco at the rig. Devang at the terminal. Theo with the empty roll of tape. Rhea's hand already on the lock. And the door opening, and the cold of the outside coming in, and two of them stepping through it into the dark window — the room going from seven to —

Five.

Five in the room. The number she trusted, dropped by two, going out a door instead of staying in. Her chest did the arithmetic before her head could stop it and the answer was wrong, was *going to be* wrong, was the first count in her life she could not promise would come back whole.

The radio crackled.

"Eyes on the approach," Marco said. "You've got it in front of you."

On channel, the city ground past — gravel, a chain-link gate, the long blue hum of the place. Cole's breath in the speaker, steady, courier-steady, the column squared to his chest. And then Russ's voice, close on the mic, low, climbing toward the thing rising in front of them in the dark.
