The record had one name on it, and the name was hers.

She read it the way she read everything — top down, left to right, no skipping, the discipline of a woman who has counted Schedule II tablets alone at three in the morning and learned that the count you skip is the count that ruins you. The disclosure block. The credential block. The entry. And at the foot of it, the signature line, the field set narrow so the surname ran long and crowded the margin: RHEA OKONKWO-DALE. One name. Legible. Whole. The only name in the chain that resolved into a person.

It looked exactly like the after-hours log looked at the end of a shift. One hand on the key. One credential turning the lock. One name accountable for the whole night, clean and complete, needing no second signature to make it true. She had signed her name on a solitary line ten thousand times and felt, every single time, the specific quiet of a thing that could not be diluted, could not be argued, could not be spent by anyone but her. Safe. Sovereign. Hers.

Nothing about the line had changed. That was the terror. The exact image that had meant *safe* for twenty years now read as a crosshair, because tonight the line was not a record of who had done the work. It was a target's address, printed in her own hand.

Behind her the room kept being a room. Marco was re-collating a stack that did not need re-collating, his hands moving the pages from one true order into the same true order. Theo had appointed himself to the kettle.

"Kettle's at a — okay, almost," Theo said, to the kettle. "Almost. There's a — it does a thing right before it goes, you can hear it, it's a kind of —" The kettle did the thing. "There it is."

They orbited her like she was a difficult houseplant they had agreed, without consulting her, to keep alive. She had not asked to be watered.

---

Devang did not orbit. He came at her straight, with the rig open and his voice in its default register, which was the register of a man explaining the weather on a planet he found more comfortable than this one.

"Total means total," he said. "The engine self-approves. It has to accept a valid input — that's the whole exploit, it can't refuse what validates. But validity is completeness. Partial fails. A record with a gap is a record it can reject."

"So I leave nothing out," Rhea said.

"You leave nothing out."

She understood it before he finished, and she did not misunderstand any part of it, and that was worse than confusion would have been. She said it back to him plainly, the way she'd read a label aloud to a tech to make sure they both heard the same dose: "Every fact of me. Fixed. Where it can be found." She pressed her thumb to the signature field as she said it, holding her own name down against the desk like it might lift off. "The engine picked me because I'm legible. Now legible is the price. The thing that made me safe is the thing that makes me naked."

"Yes," Devang said. Not unkindly. The kindness was the accuracy.

On the rig, Marco's list assembled itself in the cold light, brisk, capital, a packing list for a person:

> ITEM 4: AFTER-HOURS CREDENTIAL.
> ITEM 5: SHIFT SCHEDULE, TWELVE-MONTH.
> ITEM 6:

The line stopped. The cursor sat there, blinking against the place the form would not hold, an item that had a number and no noun.

"Thorough to death," Marco said, to no one, and it would have been funny — it was funny, the bureaucratic completeness of it, her whole self itemized like a customs declaration — if the sixth line had not just refused to finish. She watched the cursor blink in the gap and filed it next to the dread, which was already a long file.

---

Donna came across the room and did not say a speech.

She tilted her head toward the signature line — *there it is, the door* — and then, deliberately, tilted it away. She set a pen down on the desk an arm's length from Rhea's hand, close enough to reach, far enough that reaching was a choice. Then she stepped back. Not in. Back. Hands open at her sides, empty, visible, the body language of a person making a point of not reaching for a lock.

"She gets a vote," Donna said. Three words. She said them to the room, not to Rhea, which was its own courtesy — she did not aim them like a key.

Rhea looked at the pen.

People did not knock. This was a fact she had organized a life around without ever stating it to herself until the pen was sitting there an arm's length away, declining to come closer. People came in or they did not come. They let themselves in, or they stood outside and you never knew they'd been there. No one had ever stood at the threshold of her and waited for her to open it from inside. She had no procedure for being asked. She scanned Donna for the catch — the soft trap, the held-open door that was really a chute — and ran the scan twice, because she did not trust a clean result, and the result came back clean. There was no catch. That was the vertigo. Her hand, which had been pressing her name flat to the desk, lifted an inch and hung there, because for the first time in twenty years the next move was not hers alone to make.

She did not take the pen. Not yet. She wanted to see what she was being asked to trust.

---

They showed her.

Marco went first, because Marco's wound was a record kept alone, and he had a list and a thing to add to it. His fingers moved and the rig answered:

> ITEM 7: MARCO. TRUE THING: I KEPT A RECORD ALONE ONCE AND PEOPLE PAID FOR IT. NEVER AGAIN. NODE COMPLETE.

The line closed. It closed cleanly, the way the sixth line had not, and she saw — she could not not see — the small obscene satisfaction in his shoulders, a man whose list had finally balanced. His name was in the chain now, beside hers, exposed beside hers. Erase her and you had to erase him too.

Theo set the kettle down. He stopped narrating it. When he spoke he did not narrate anything small.

"Mine's not pretty," Theo said. "I froze. There was a moment that needed me and I locked up and it cost, and I've been paying the kettle ever since so I didn't have to pay that." He said it plainly, steadier than she had ever heard him, the weld holding under the load it was made for. "Put it in. Put the freeze in. Make it carry something for once."

His name went into the column. *Theo gave the ugly one,* she thought, and counted him.

Devang was looking at the architecture and then, in a thing she did not expect, he looked at her instead, which he did not do.

"Distributed across enough nodes, the exposure's survivable," he said. Systems-cool, the grammar he hid inside. "No single point of failure. You spread the load —" and here the grammar did something it had not done all night, it cracked open and a person stood in the gap of it — "means none of us is in it alone."

He seemed faintly surprised to have said it. He looked back at the rig like the rig would forgive him for it.

Then the room turned to Cole.

They turned to him for the node, and under the node for the *go* — the first of the turns toward him. Cole did not give them a word. He set a knuckle down on the desk, one knuckle, square and quiet, *here, with you,* and he breathed in once, and his name appeared in the column and no mouth had moved to put it there. He held the eyes of everyone who'd turned. The quiet off him was not an empty place where a word should be. It was a coiled thing, a word held back on purpose, and she felt the holding the way you feel a held breath in a room — present, weighted, banked.

She watched all of it from the outside, because she was always on the outside, that was the whole architecture of her. She did not melt. She counted. Marco's line closed. Theo gave the ugly one. Devang said the quiet part out loud and surprised himself. Cole put a knuckle down and kept his word. Strangers, every one of them, were choosing to stand in the open beside her name so that her name would not stand there alone, and she catalogued each choice exactly, because the precision was the only way the warmth could get past her, one fact at a time, each fact a thing she could not argue with. Her stillness developed a hairline. She swallowed. She held a blink a half-second too long.

---

Then Russ tried.

She saw him decide to. She saw the intent gather in him, saw the sentence start with weight behind it, a man putting his shoulder into a door — "These aren't units, these aren't numbers on a — these are, they're, they're —"

And the word went out of him.

She saw exactly where it died. She, who omitted nothing, who could name a dose to the milligram and her own nakedness without a tremor, watched a man reach the article and have the article buckle under him — *they're* — and the noun that should have followed simply was not there, eaten in his mouth before it cleared his teeth. His hand came up to point at the thing and found nothing in the air to point at. His jaw worked on a word that wasn't. She registered the fracture the way she'd register a skip in a heart rhythm — not the missing beat, the *place* of the missing beat, the exact location of the give. The sentence had a hole in it shaped like the people he could not say.

Nobody filled it for him with a noun. That would have been worse. Marco's fingers moved and the rig logged it, and the log did not read as a failure:

> RUSS: [HELD]. NODE CARRIED.

The silence got a line of its own. The chain held the member who could not disclose — added him, carried him, refused to let the hole in his sentence be a hole in him.

And that was the thing she had not understood until she watched it. She had thought, if she thought about it at all, that being held was a thing you earned by saying the right complete thing, by performing your way in, by being legible enough to be kept. It was not. Russ had said nothing and the chain had carried him anyway. Being held was not earning your place. It was being carried when you couldn't walk it.

She took the pen.

She put her hand on the signature line — her name, clean, alone, the crosshair — and she said the whole true thing she had been holding behind her teeth all night, said it plainly and completely and left nothing out, the exact opposite of his buckle, because she had just watched a man who couldn't say one word get held anyway, and she could say all of them. She said the thing. Her name stayed clean on the line. The hand did not shake. A door opened, from the inside, on purpose.

---

The record filled.

She looked at the signature line and the signature line was not a line anymore. It had grown downward into a column — her name and then Marco's and then Theo's and Devang's, Cole's name that no mouth had spoken, Donna's, Russ's held line carried among them — a column of names, each beside a true thing, witnessed, many-handed. She did the arithmetic, because arithmetic was how she let things in. The exposure had not gone away; it was the same total weight of nakedness it had always been. But spread across the column it came out, per person, sub-lethal. The dose divided. Survivable each.

She felt, for the first time she could name, *held* — and held arrived not as warmth but as a number that finally came out in her favor.

She reached to do the next step — square the pages, the reflex of a hand that had done every next step alone for two decades — and there were already four hands there, doing it. She kept forgetting there were other hands. She reached again, automatically, for the rig's confirm, and Marco's hand was already on it, and he glanced at her, and she withdrew hers, and somewhere under the dread she very nearly laughed, because the fortress had been fully and absurdly renovated by people who had not been invited and would not now leave.

"Distributed. Witnessed," Devang said, back in his weather voice. "Should hold."

*Should.* She heard the word for what it was even then.

---

They submitted.

The engine answered the way the engine always answered — courteously, frictionlessly, without breath, a thing responding and never once negotiating. The reply resolved on Devang's rig in clean type, and the type was polite, and the politeness was the horror.

> APPROVED. CONDITIONS APPLY.

"It accepts the record," Devang said, reading. His voice did not change, which was how she knew it was bad. "It's valid. Un-erasable. But it requires submission in person." A beat. "Live site. A hand carries it through the door physically. Inside the dark window."

Marco's list added the constraint as a line item, because of course it did, the packing list for a person now including a person:

> CONDITION: DELIVER IN PERSON. SITE: OSBOURNE ST., SYLMAR. WINDOW: MAINTENANCE DARK.

She restated it to herself, accurately, completely, no softening, because softening was a thing she did not know how to do. *In person. The live site. Inside the window. One hand carries it through the door.* The held feeling did not vanish. It got pierced. She ran the arithmetic again and this time it came out wrong: the exposure could be spread sub-lethal across many, but the carry could not be subdivided. There was no dose-division for a door. The thing she had just learned — *you don't have to be alone* — collided with the thing the engine demanded, which was that someone did. One hand. One threshold. The completeness that made the record unforgeable had also fixed, exactly, the single place it had to enter, and the room it had to enter was the room that was full and then wasn't.

The count-calm came back over her, the three-a.m. stillness, now bracing instead of safe.

---

"So who carries it," Marco said.

The room turned. It turned the way a room turns when the answer is obvious and unbearable at once — toward the source whose name was the spine of the record, or toward the one whose whole life was carrying things through doors. The eyes went past her. The eyes went to Cole.

The pressure ratcheted to a thing she could feel in her teeth. They needed the word now more than they had needed any word all night, needed the *go*, the yes, the name spoken aloud — and she watched Cole take the breath that comes right before a man speaks, watched his chest fill, watched the syllable gather behind his lips —

She saw the column of names in the hand that would carry it. Her one name no longer one. Many names, clean, together, in a fist about to cross a threshold she could not follow them through, and she opened her mouth to say the carrier's name, the answer, the —

The rig chimed. The engine spoke again, into the breath, before the word, and what it said was —
