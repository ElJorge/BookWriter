### Beat 1 — Read.
**Structural summary:** No time elapsed from Ch1's dead screen. The phone is in Marco's hand on the train (first cut off-screen, hand up into face). Held silence — then under Russ's last message, **"Delivered" flickers to "Read."** The thread detonates out of paralysis into a race. Nobody knows who opened it. Marco — the punctuator who couldn't type at all in Ch1 Beat 7 — finds his words by finding a task.

**Character annotations:**
- **Marco** (lead)
  - Subtext: The Read receipt is unbearable *because* it's hope with no content — someone is on the other side and won't speak. His Ch1-Beat-7 paralysis (no words) breaks the instant there's a *procedure* to run; coordination is how he refuses to feel afraid.
  - Unspoken want: For the "Read" to mean Russ. (Stated want: to organize everyone before panic wins.)
  - **Stress-state voice shift:** Archivist re-boots as *incident commander before Devang can.* His first coherent text is a **numbered triage list, capitals back, em-dashes returning** — the typographical signal that Marco has a job again. But watch the em-dash: when his list touches *who could have read it,* the dash trails off (he can't number "is he alive").
  - Voice cadence: "Okay. 1. Someone read it. 2. That's not nothing. 3. Who is near him — physically near him — RIGHT NOW." Punctuation fully intact; that intactness is the cope.
  - Physical expression: On his feet on the moving train, thumb suddenly fast, the frozen hand from Ch1 unlocked by purpose.
- **Theo**
  - Subtext: Reads "Read" as pure hope for one line because hope is a performance he can do reflexively — then the floor opens when he realizes Read-with-no-reply might be *worse.*
  - **Stress-state voice shift:** The bit fires first — overcelebrates the Read — then **cracks on landing.** Lowercase to a single broken CAP. The joke arrives a half-second before the dread and gets eaten by it. This is the chapter's first "joke is the flinch."
  - Voice cadence: "HE READ IT / he read it that means he's— / ...wait / why isn't he typing / theo why isn't he typing" (he says his own name; the performer addressing himself, audience gone).
- **Cole**
  - **Stress-state voice shift:** Already shifting from words to motion. One or zero texts in this beat — present only as a silence the others haven't noticed yet. Plant it: he's reading, not typing. (Don't pay it; Beat 4 reveals he's in the truck.)
- **Devang**
  - Subtext: A read receipt is a *signal,* and signals are his native language — but a signal with no payload is a system in an unknown state, which terrifies him more than a clean failure.
  - **Stress-state voice shift:** Procedural shell snaps on. "ack. read at—" and he *timestamps the read receipt* like a log entry. The deadpan jargon lands funny against the terror.
  - Voice cadence: "ok. someone has device access. that's a data point. it is not a good or bad data point yet. everyone hold."

**Relationship dynamic:** Marco vs. Devang in a near-collision for incident command — both reach for control as cope, and Marco gets there first (his arc: the relied-on one, relied-on again). Theo orbits both, the emotional barometer cracking.
**Dialogue direction:** Open on held silence + the visual of the flicker. Theo's over-celebration is the comedy flinch; let it fire and die in three short bursts. Marco's numbered list is the reboot — capitals and periods are the *signal* he's functional. Devang timestamping the read is the deadpan. End the beat the instant all four realize Read-no-reply is the worse outcome — funny, then cold.

### Beat 4 — Cole Drives (The Silence That Moves)
**Structural summary:** The reveal: **Cole is already on the 405.** While the others hunted and dialed, the mute one got in the truck. No speech — he drops a live **location share** into the Chain and a single text: a distance. The other three watch his blue dot move toward Simi Valley, and the chapter reorders. **Character annotations:**
- **Cole** (driving, near-silent — this is his chapter-turn and the bible's deepest payoff)
  - Subtext: The man who never finished a hard thing, who was mute with terror at the end of Ch1, has converted that exact muteness into the one thing he *can* do — drive. He can't say it, so he drives it. Words were never his medium for fear; motion is.
  - Unspoken want: To not be the fraud who drops the baby — to *finish* something, for once, at the highest stakes there are.
  - **Stress-state voice shift (the chapter's central inversion):** His silence is no longer absence — it's ACTION, rendered as a **live location dot** (his real "dialogue") plus **clipped voice-to-text distance-bursts.** He speaks only in numbers: "55 min." / "405." / "moving." He does NOT get articulate or sincere — that would break the arc. His refusal to say anything but distances is the most eloquent thing in the chapter.
  - Voice cadence: voice-to-text, so it's weirdly clean of his usual typos — the machine punctuating for a man who never punctuates is its own small horror. "On the 405." / "52 minutes." / "Don't stop texting me. I can't text back." (That last is the one allowed crack — and I'd hold even *that* for Beat 5/6; see below.)
  - Physical expression: Both hands on the wheel, phone in the dash mount, doing 90 in a 65, jaw set, the surf-calm gone, a man finishing a hard thing for the first time in his life.
- **The other three** (watching the dot, feeding him)
  - **Stress-state voice shift:** Their terror projects onto Cole's moving dot — they narrate it, beg it, joke at it. This is where the gallows comedy survives the drive.
  - **Theo** (still trapped behind the bar): live-narrates Cole's dot like a man calling a horse race he has his whole heart on.   - **Devang:** treats the dot as a monitored process. "cole's eta is holding. i'm watching it. it's the only green thing on my screen." (Load-bearing irony: "green" as in healthy-status, and as in the only hope.)

**Relationship dynamic:** Three men pouring their whole terror into one silent moving point — the inverse of Ch1's thread that always answered itself. Cole's silence now *holds* the others rather than frightening them, because it's pointed at Russ. The father/son Cole→Russ dynamic inverts: the son drives to save the father.
**Dialogue direction:** Cole speaks ONLY in distances and route-facts — voice-to-text, machine-clean, devastating. Do not let him get articulate. The location dot is his dialogue; the others' narration of it is the comedy and the love. Theo's horse-race narration is the gallows-comedy peak of the beat — let it run, then let the "if you die too" land and curdle. **Where comedy goes quiet:** save Cole's single non-distance text for the chapter's late stretch (Beat 5 or 6) so that when it finally comes — one human sentence after twenty miles of numbers — it guts. Don't spend it here.
