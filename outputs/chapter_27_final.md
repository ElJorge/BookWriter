# Chapter 27 — The Count

She counted them by the face, the way she always had.

Coffee steam off the folding table. The roll-up door three-quarters down, cold air sliding under it across the concrete and up her shins. A borrowed room — somebody's day-room, somebody's loading office, not theirs; they had no theirs anymore. Donna stood at the lip of it with her hands in her pockets and ran the count the only way she trusted, which was no clipboard, no roster, just her eyes going chin to chin and her gut keeping the tally.

The number came back wrong.

She ran it again. Marco, off in the corner with his screen. Devang beside him. Theo, mid-sentence at the urn. Cole by the wall where the light didn't reach, still, exact. Russ at the table. And then the others. Rhea, sitting with a cup she hadn't been handed by anyone Donna had deployed. Two more she hadn't sent for. The number kept landing high.

For years her count had come back one short. She had built a whole nervous system around bracing for it — the small drop in the stomach before the tally closed, the reflex of looking once more at the place where somebody should have been and wasn't, ever since before the dock, ever since the one she hadn't reached in time. The muscle for that was strong. It fired now, on schedule, and found nothing to grab. The count was over. Surplus. Her reflex couldn't parse it; it just kept counting, like a tongue working a tooth that had stopped hurting.

"Count," she said, to herself, and did it a third time with a finger this time, which was the tell, which meant something was off.

Still over.

Theo turned with the pot. "I did the coffee for fourteen."

"There's nine of us."

"I know." He didn't apologize for it. He poured.

She let the surplus stand a beat, annoyed in a way she couldn't have named without it coming out warm, and a foreman who couldn't get a clean count was a foreman with a problem. The problem this morning was too many. She wasn't built for that problem. She filed it and moved.

"Marco. Where's the record."

She read it standing, the screen tilted toward her over Marco's shoulder, the blue of it cold on his face. A man who'd been told for most of his life that nothing he kept would ever be allowed to stand, and here it stood. He just laid the planks down, numbered, em-dashed, each one nailed flush, and didn't say a word about that either. She let him have it that way.

Her eyes went down the pattern to the count of the gone. To the place where the word would go. The room that had been full and then quietly wasn't.

She stopped there.

She did not say it. She moved her eyes past it the way you step over a hole you've memorized, and the gap sat where it always sat, the way it always had — a place gone quiet, the going hidden.

"Redundant?" she said.

Devang didn't look up from his terminal. "Three sites. Two I don't control, which is the point of them. If somebody burns this building down with all of us in it, the record's up in eleven seconds somewhere I can't be made to find." He typed. "Uptime's the highest I've ever shipped."

Nobody said the thing it actually was. Donna heard it anyway, and so did the room, and nobody called it love out loud. They let it sit there being uptime.

---

The work wasn't done. There was the one left.

No name they could say. No face. No word of his on anything anywhere — that was the man, the whole of him: a machine built so that no hand would ever rest on a single thing, his least of all. He'd slipped at the end. Located. Named for what he was. Not caught. You couldn't catch what had no handle.

Except.

"Okay, so," Theo said, and the room sagged a little because Theo was off. "So here's the thing, here's the actual thing, because I kept — listen — I kept seeing the same — no, hang on — the erasures, right, all of them, clean, surgical, no trace, except there's this one — this little — see it's a partial, it's a smudge really, it's nothing, except it's here, and it's here, and look, it's here too, same one, same ridge pattern, three years apart, four jobs, one—"

"Theo."

"—one mark, it's a fingerprint, it's a human fingerprint, on a machine that exists so no human—"

"Theo. Land it."

He landed it. Hands flat on the table over four sheets he'd laid side by side. "It's his. It's the only one he ever left."

The room had gone quiet around him. Donna looked at the over-talker who could not stop reaching until the thing lined up, and she did not stop him, because she'd read that hand a while ago — the not-shutting-up was the tool, a man reaching across a gap nobody else would cross. She let it run.

A god with a smudge.

---

"So we end him," Theo said. The talk had drained out of him; what was left had teeth. "We've got the record. We could put him in the count of the gone. Do it to him. He made a hundred people into a — into a blank. We make him one. Easy. Justice."

It would have felt like justice. That was the trouble with it. It was right there in her hands, the method — she of all of them knew exactly how you made a man gone, how you emptied a place around him until he was a hole with no edges. She could feel the pull of it like a current at the back of the knees.

She held still.

"No," she said.

She didn't argue it. A foreman didn't argue; she ruled, and the rule was a work order, not a speech.

"We don't take a hand off." She nodded at the record. "We put one on."

Theo opened his mouth. Closed it.

"Connect him," she said. "Marco. Attach the trace. Fix it to the pattern. Link it, name it for what it is. Don't bury him. Reach him."

She watched the entry close. Somewhere out there a man who'd made unreachability his whole religion was, at this second, reachable — not grabbed, not cuffed, not vanished. Attached. Known. A trace that had spent four jobs sliding frictionless off every surface had finally found the one surface it could not leave, because it had been written there, by hands, on a record that didn't let go.

Whether the world that was now watching put him in a cell was the world's problem. The record forced the question and would not let it drop. That was enough. It had to be; it was what they had.

"He's known," she said. Flat. A fact, not a flag.

"We caught a god with a smudge," Devang said, "and the win is paperwork."

"The win is usually paperwork," Donna said, and let one breath out, and that was the whole celebration.

---

She made her last sweep of the room the way she counted — face by face, but deeper this time, not bodies. Belonging.

Russ at the table. Somebody from the surplus, one of the two she hadn't sent for, leaned in and asked him low, "The ones from the cold room — the—" and Russ went to answer and the word he reached for wasn't there. The article hung off the end of him with nothing under it. His mouth worked at a noun that had been eaten out of him a long time ago and stayed eaten, and what fell out instead was a work-order synonym, flat and wrong and the only thing he had — "the personnel." He said *the personnel*, and his face did the thing it did, and nobody at the table filled it for him.

That was the company. Not that they cleared him — they didn't; the brand stayed, the man who opened the door, it would never come off him. Just that he wasn't alone holding the hole anymore. He reached across the table for the stack of sheets to square them and three other hands were already on the stack, and he let his rest on top, a hand among hands, and Donna clocked that and moved on.

Cole, by the wall. He stepped to the table and set down a cable run, coiled clean, exactly where the next job would need it — no word, he had no more words to spend, he'd spent his one and faulted the engine with it and that was all the speech the man owed anyone for the rest of his life. He set it down. And Devang, without being asked, without looking up, reached over and pulled it into reach and said, "Yeah. That's the one I need," answering the thing Cole hadn't said, and the room shifted half a degree toward him, around him, the way a chain takes a load. He was heard.

Rhea, with her cup. Donna caught her eye. "You're on the chain. Verification. Clean reads, you flag, you don't fix — you flag."

"I can do that," Rhea said. Whole sentence. Name clean, never near the gap, never inside it. A thing that had been done-to, doing now. A link.

---

Then the record again. The count of the gone, and the place in it where the word would go.

She stood at it the way she'd stood at it the whole time — the same silence, the same blank she'd memorized, the same hole she stepped over. She didn't fill it now any more than she'd filled it before. She didn't reach for the noun.

She held the place.

She left the slot in the count uncrossed. Unnamed and unclosed — a seat at the table with no card on it, kept. The man whose whole life's work was emptying rooms had made this gap; she stood in front of it and did the other thing, the only thing she knew how to do, which was not to let it close. Same blank. She read it, and this time it did not read as loss. It read as kept.

She didn't say any of that. She nudged the roll-up another half-inch up off the concrete instead, so the room would stay porous, so it could be heard into, the opposite of a sealed room, and went back to the table.

---

"Run it," she said, and ran it herself, face by face, one more time.

Crew. The new one — Rhea, counted in. The two from the surplus. The held place at the table that she did not cross out and did not name. It came back whole. It came back over.

The count that had come back one short for the better part of her life came back full and then some, and she stood there with the surplus in her hands like a thing she didn't have a pocket for.

"We're all unemployable now, you know that," Theo said. "Marked. Every one of us. Can't go back, can't go quiet, can't get hired anywhere that runs a check." He was grinning. "We're radioactive."

"Permanently," Devang said.

"Together, though," Rhea said.

They laughed, and it had cost in it, and it was true on both ends — no theirs to go back to, no hidden lives, no clean exits, and the laugh held anyway.

Donna ran the number a last time and it came back over, again, stubborn, and she stopped recounting. She let it stand high. "Can't get a clean count in this outfit," she said, and the steel in it cracked a hairline, once, the most she'd ever let show, "and I find I don't much care."

Then the foreman came back. "Marco. Lock the record. Devang, watch the mirrors. Cole — good run. Everybody, we work tonight."

The room moved. Hands on the stack, on the cable, on the cups. Cold air still came under the door, and the coffee was bad, and they had nowhere to sleep and nothing they could call theirs and a brand on every one of them that wouldn't come off.

She looked at the roll-up door, open a half-inch off the concrete, and left it that way, so the room would stay full.
