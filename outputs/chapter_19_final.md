# Chapter 19 — Final

The green node sat in the middle of his map and breathed.

Slow pulse, in and out, a steady friendly green he'd been reading for two hours now the way you read a face across a table. Devang had a name for the rest of the board — the crew strung out as a mesh, each of them an endpoint he could ping and get an honest return; comms a handshake he'd rebuilt by hand after the blackout dropped every connection at once, all of them gone dark and then, one at a time, back; Rhea a stable line holding from the dead pharmacy window, low jitter, no drops, the cleanest endpoint he had. But the green node didn't get a protocol name. The green node got *buddy.*

"You've been chatty since the lights came back," he told it, under his breath, fingers resting on the one good keyboard, the borrowed monitors throwing cool blue across his hands. "Little eager. Like you missed me." The fans hummed. The node pulsed. "Which is insane. Nodes don't miss. Write that down."

Nobody wrote it down. That was the joke, and there was no one in the room to land it on, which was its own kind of data.

Here was the thing he wasn't saying, the thing the protocol-talk was stacked against like sandbags against a door: the green meant *alive, reachable, mine.* He'd been treating it as a someone — a counterparty at the far end of a connection, a hand on a keyboard somewhere, a person with a latency and a mood — because that was the only fight he knew. Find the human. Model the human. Beat the human. He leaned toward the green like it was a mouth about to speak. Caught himself. Leaned anyway.

"Rhea." He keyed her line. "You still with me."

"I'm here." Her voice came flat and total down the wire, no color on it, nothing held back behind it. "Nothing's moved. The window's still dark."

"Good. Stay an endpoint. Best one I've got." He pulled up the trace he'd been building off her account, fields half-populated, a dependency tree with one branch he couldn't resolve. "I need you to say the thing the event ate. Before the line cut. You started to tell me what the hand did to your name and then it was gone. Say it again. All of it. Slow."

A pause, but not a flinching one. With Rhea the pause was just her making sure she gave him the whole thing.

"I signed off," she said. "Plain disclosure, complete, the way I always do. It's supposed to go to a person for the counter-signature. Somebody reads it, somebody signs it back. That's the rule." A beat. "It came back signed before I'd lifted my hand. Both times. Faster than anybody reads. There was nobody there. No second pair of eyes in the window. I watched the whole thing and there was nobody there."

Devang's hands moved while she talked, turning her account into fields — timestamp, hop, return — and each clean detail she handed him got a small nod, because she never made him guess, never made him fill a gap she'd left, she just gave him the whole shape of it and let him build.

A cold draft moved through him at *came back signed before I'd lifted my hand,* and he rerouted it on reflex into something survivable.

"Okay," he said. "So it round-trips. Fine. Round-trips need somewhere to go. Somewhere has a someone — a proxy, a delegate, a guy with a macro and no soul, signing a thousand of these in his sleep. Fast just means a fast person." He was already typing toward the far end of the hop, hunting the chair. "Everybody's got a someone. Hang on. I'm going to go meet yours."

---

He followed the counter-signature back along the wire, hop to hop, expecting at the end of it the things a person leaves: a terminal that idles, a session that times out, a cursor blinking somewhere that means *someone is typing back.* He'd find the chair. There's always a chair.

There was no chair.

There was a function. He read it three times before he let himself read it for what it was. It took a valid credential event as input. It emitted an authorization as output. In between there was nothing — no operator, no gate, no review queue, no one to wake at three in the morning, no one who ever decided. Valid in. Approved out. The whole erasure ran itself, a hand with no person attached to it, signing and signing because signing was the only thing it knew how to return.

He looked for the cursor that meant *someone is there.* There wasn't one. There never had been one. The green node hadn't been a counterparty holding a slow conversation with him. It had been a return statement, answering his pings because answering was all it did, the same green whether he watched it or didn't, whether he begged it or threatened it or turned the screen off and walked away.

"You can't even—" The dry one climbed his throat — *you can't even leave it a voicemail* — and died there, because the shape of the thing closed over him cold and clean. You cannot lie to a function. You cannot scare a return value. You cannot out-human a thing that has no human in it to out. Every weapon he owned needed a *who,* and he had just walked the whole wire down to the bottom and found there was no who. There never was. The thing he'd called *buddy* had never once heard him.

It was never listening. It was only ever responding.

He leaned away from the green now. It pulsed on, exactly as friendly as before, unchanged, which was the most frightening thing on the board.

---

The chat stuttered to life on a surviving screen, lines arriving in Marco's hard staccato.

> marco: CROSS-REF RUNNING. YOU WANT TO KNOW IF WE'VE SEEN THIS BEFORE. WE HAVE.
> marco: PATTERN CONFIRMED — 1) APPROVAL PRESENT 2) APPROVER ABSENT 3) ORIGIN FIELD —

The lines stopped.

Devang waited. Scrolled down for the rest of it. There wasn't any rest of it.

"Origin field, what," he said aloud, to the room, to the earpiece, to nobody. "Marco. What's after it. Marco." He read the dead line again and found he couldn't finish it for him either; the field just wouldn't resolve into a noun, the way none of them did anymore, the way one cold-room field on his board had refused for days to become a name. "It's not loading. Marco, the third one's not loading."

> marco: IT LOADED. THAT IS THE FIELD. THERE'S NOTHING IN IT BY DESIGN.
> marco: 1) APPROVAL EXISTS 2) NO APPROVER EXISTS 3) NO AUTHOR EXISTS. THE HOLE ISN'T A GAP. IT'S THE SPEC.

"Somebody built it to have no builder," Devang said, and felt the floor tilt the other way — because *built* meant a builder, and a builder was a who, a who he could hunt, the first foothold he'd had since the chair turned out empty. "If it was built, then there's a—"

In his ear Donna's breath went still, the kind of still that's already a question.

---

"Then who are we even fighting," Donna said. Clipped, level, the cue that carried the spine the rest of them had misplaced. "If the machine signs. Then what's *he.*"

Devang didn't get to answer. A line opened — not one he'd opened — and a voice came onto it, courteous, frictionless, no breath moving under the words.

"Everything is in order," it said.

Devang's eyes went straight to the response-time counter, the oldest reflex he had: ping the endpoint, read the latency, infer the human, because people hesitate, people breathe, people leave a gap where the thinking goes. The counter sat clean. Too clean. No jitter at all.

"Who am I talking to," he said.

"There's nothing here that requires you," the voice answered, the same smooth surface, helpful and located nowhere, declining to be a man without ever declining anything.

No jitter. He watched the counter the way you'd watch a polygraph that won't move. People jitter. That was either the calmest man alive or it was the man-shaped port on the same box — an interface bolted onto the process, returning the identical seamless output whether or not anyone stood behind it. He pinged it for a tell, any tell, the breath-gap where a person thinks, and got back nothing he could distinguish from a generated string. Architect or interface. The builder of the engine or a courtesy soldered to its outside. He could not tell, in any grammar he owned, whether he was talking to a person or to another endpoint of the same thing — and the worst of it, the thing that tilted the ground all the way over, was the creeping sense that there might be no difference, and that the difference not existing was exactly the point.

The line closed. No goodbye. The counter went idle.

"He didn't gloat," Devang said, to the quiet. "That's the part. A man gloats. That didn't even—" He let it go.

---

He sat with his hands off the keyboard and let the whole terrible shape stand up in front of him, full height.

His entire life he'd been the network brain with no networked solution. There's no API for a locked door. There's no endpoint for grief, no handshake with a man who won't pick up, no schema that parses the thing a person does when there's nothing left to do. He'd spent the whole of this so far pinging walls that wouldn't answer, and every wall had been human-shaped, and that had been the wound: that he reduced everything to a system, and the world kept handing him problems that had no system in them at all.

And now.

Now the enemy was a system. Finally, actually a system — input, output, spec, the whole indifferent machinery of it. Which was his nightmare made flesh, the no-one-home he'd dreaded since the chair came up empty. And it was, in the same breath, the first fight on this entire planet that was shaped exactly like him.

Because a system has a spec. A spec has rules. Rules have inputs they're built to take and edge-cases they never thought of and things — *things* — they are required to accept whether they like it or not, which they can't, because they don't like anything. You can't reason with it. So he'd stop trying. You don't reason with a spec. You *read* it. And then you feed it.

His hands came back to the one good keyboard, and the lean returned — not toward the green this time, toward the spec, toward the rules — and a breath he'd been holding since the first locked door went out of him at last.

"For the first time since this started," he said, low, almost a laugh, the dry exhausted kind, "the world's speaking my language." The green pulsed. "It's saying something terrible. But it's saying it to *me.* In syntax I can finally parse. God help me, this is the one I was built for."

On the edge of his vision Russ shifted. Devang caught it without turning all the way — Russ gone very still in a way that cost him, a hand opening and closing once at his side, his mouth starting on something.

"I was the—" Russ said, and the noun didn't come. The article hung there in the air, naked, with nothing to land on, and the sentence just stopped, the way the cold-room field stopped, the way the third line stopped. Russ had been the key. Spent on the access job, read once and returned, no different to the engine than Rhea's sign-off was — an input, consumed, discarded — and now he was watching the machinery that spent him run itself on without him.

Devang clocked the buckle and stayed outside it. Some things you don't ping. He let Russ have the broken sentence whole.

---

"A system that must accept valid input," Devang said, up out of the chair now, drawing the loop in the air with both hands — *input here, accept here, turn here* — "can be fed something it cannot refuse. It *has* to honor a valid sign-off. The spec can't tell the difference between compliance and an attack — that's the whole game. So we don't break the lock. We don't find the man. We give it an input its own rules force it to take, and we make the taking the thing that turns it."

"What kind of input." Donna, holding the crew to a target.

"The one kind it can't get anywhere else." He turned toward Rhea's line. "A disclosure that's perfectly valid *and* perfectly complete. Omits nothing. The system reads everything; a man of omissions can never produce that, never, his whole nature is the gaps. But a plain, total, honest one—"

"Then I tell it everything," Rhea said, even and clean and threaded now with something dry and hard. "I'm good at that. It's the only thing I've ever been good at."

"It would accept her," Theo started, fast, the old over-talk spooling up, "and then we'd have a foothold inside the authorization layer, which means in principle we could—" and he heard himself, audibly, and reeled it in. "Okay. One thing. Just the one. What does it cost *her.* If it accepts her and then we turn it. What happens to Rhea."

Nobody answered for a second. Devang didn't, because he didn't have it, and because Theo had narrowed a spiral down to the one question that mattered and that deserved the silence.

> marco: SPEC SAYS MUST-ACCEPT ON VALID — CONFIRMED. NO HUMAN OVERRIDE LISTED. WHICH MEANS —

The crew came to the lip of it together. The kind of moment that needs a *go,* a single word to push them over, and every one of them looked the same direction at once — toward the one mouth in the room whose word would be the word.

Cole didn't open it.

A knuckle came down on the table instead. One tap. One breath in, one out, slow, that the room read as *go* and that was not a word and would not be scored as one. The silence where the word should have been had weight now, a held charge, and nobody named it, and it was — almost. Almost enough. Devang felt the whole room take the knuckle in place of the word and lean on it anyway.

"That's the shape," he said. "We don't have the thing yet. But that's the shape."

"Then that's the work." Donna, clipped, forward. "First shape. We build it."

---

He didn't build it. Not yet. But he could prove the concept, and proving it would settle the only axiom the whole plan stood on: a system just responds.

He wrote the probe small. Minimal. Deniable — a single test input, the lightest possible question put to the spec, *will you accept what your own rules say you must.* He expected a response, because a response was all the thing had ever given anyone. That was the entire foundation under the hope he'd just found: no attention, no will, no one home, only a function returning whether or not anyone watched.

He sent it. One keystroke.

He watched the green for the answer.

The green answered. And then it did the other thing — the thing that wasn't in the spec, the thing no rule he'd read allowed, a change of state with nothing human behind it that nonetheless read, horribly, exactly like *attention.* It didn't just return to him. It turned. The slow friendly pulse that had meant *alive, reachable, mine* for two hours leaned the other way across the board and meant the inverse of itself now, meant *seen,* meant the thing he'd been watching had this whole time been—

He went still the way you go still when something you were sure was a picture blinks. Leaned toward it one last time, the old affection gone all the way over into dread.

"That's not—" His voice came out wrong. "It can't change state like that, that's not in the spec, that means there's more than one of them, or it's—"
