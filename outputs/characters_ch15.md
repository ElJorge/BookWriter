## Annotated Beats

### Beat 1 — Hands on the Bar (the POV cracks open)
**Structural summary:** After-close at Theo's. Russ is physically out, at the bar, hands flat and holding nothing, held by nothing. He keeps clocking the door behind the bar — openable from either side now. He reaches for the feeling of being rescued and it won't arrive. He watches the crew the way a handler watches assets, then catches himself with no other gear. POV cracks open: first sustained interiority of a man who's been a held object the whole book.

**Character annotations:**
- **RUSS (POV)**
  - Subtext: Beneath the flatness, panic with no surface — he has an *inside* now and it's the most uncomfortable room he's stood in. He is performing "rescued man" for himself and the performance won't take.
  - Stated want vs. unspoken want: Stated (to himself) — *sit still, you're out, be done.* Unspoken — to find the switch for the feeling everyone assumes he must have, and the dawning that the limb is dead.
  - Voice cadence: Interior runs on dispatch grammar — he assesses the room as a job. "Two exits. One asset wiping the same glass. Lead at four o'clock, cueing the others down." Then the catch: *that's people. Those are people.* NEW range: a thin dry note now that he's a subject — he can be wry about his own malfunction ("apparently you reach for rescue like a light switch and apparently the bulb's gone").
  - Physical expression: Hands FLAT — the open-image. He turns one palm up, looks at it like a tool he's been handed and doesn't know the use of. Clocks the door twice. Does NOT move toward it.
- **DONNA** (background): Cueing the others down — a hand-flat *settle* gesture, a look that lands and moves on. No full sentence yet.
- **THEO** (background): Wiping the same glass. The repetition is the tell — the over-talk hasn't started; the body is stuck in a loop instead.
- **COLE** (background): Still, watching Russ. Not intruding. Plant for later.

**Relationship dynamic:** Russ vs. the crew as OBSERVER vs. observed — except inverted from the whole book: for the first time the watcher is the one being watched (Cole). The room is keeping itself gentle around him and he reads the gentleness as a handling op, because that's the only lens he owns.
**Dialogue direction:** Keep Beat 1 nearly wordless in SPOKEN terms — let the prose carry the crack. Any line aloud should be small and banal (someone slides him a glass). The dread is in the interiority's unfamiliarity. Make the prose itself feel like furniture under sheets — clean, careful, a half-beat too aware of itself. Plant BOTH the free hands and the either-side door as the open-rhyme; narrator never flags it as an image.

**Character annotations:**
- **DONNA**

  - Stated vs. unspoken want: Stated — to tell him the brand plainly. Unspoken — to test whether her thesis survives Movement Two now that the enemy can run it too; to not be the one who hands Russ back to the machine, while knowing she might be.

**Dialogue direction:** Donna's one full sentence is the chapter's pivot of theme — keep it spare, no comfort, no flourish. Russ says little aloud; the conversion of wound-to-key happens in interiority. Let the silence after Donna's sentence do the work.

**Character annotations:**
- **RUSS (POV)**

  - Stated vs. unspoken want: Stated — to say their names, even just to the alley, even just once, as a debt paid. Unspoken — absolution; to prove to himself the eraser was THEIRS so he isn't the one who keeps erasing the dead.

**Relationship dynamic:** Russ alone with his own grammar; Cole as silent witness who, uniquely, gets it. The two most word-starved men in the book, one who can't say and one who won't — paralleled without a line spoken.
**Dialogue direction:** NO character prints the cold-room noun. Keep it strictly euphemism + buckled article, rendered as Russ's failed reaching. This must DEEPEN: make the reader feel the eraser as self-inflicted, a survival tool that outlived the danger. The threshold pays the open-image forward (door crossable both ways, crossed neither). Hold the comedy OUT of this beat deliberately — its absence is felt, and the gallows register snaps back in Beat 5. ### Beat 6 — The Handler on the Wrong Side (the second person in the machine)
**Structural summary:** Russ realizes what the coordinates/credential/name ARE: a SECOND person now in the collector's machine, being spent the way Russ was. An echo of his capture, inverted — Russ is now OUTSIDE it. He'd have to do for a stranger what Donna did for him: pull a person off a dock. Except the method that pulled HIM is the dead trick. And the stranger is no one to him — not crew, not family. The handler's whole life was routing problems AWAY from himself; here's one he could simply not pick up. He feels the choice arrive and feels himself, horribly, already reaching for it.

**Character annotations:**
- **RUSS (POV)**
  - Subtext: The thematic hinge. The handler always stood OUTSIDE other people's traps and fixed them; now standing outside means abandoning someone to one. The vertigo: a debt to a stranger he could legitimately refuse — and the refusal is RIGHT THERE, clean, available, and he's already not taking it.
  - Stated vs. unspoken want: Stated — *this isn't mine. No name, no relation, no obligation. Walk.* Unspoken — he's already routing the job (entry, cover, the brand-as-key) before he's admitted he's taking it; the reach precedes the decision. THIS is the survivor-debt generalizing — he couldn't save the cold-room people, so a stranger in the same machine becomes the debt he CAN attempt.
  - Voice cadence: Interior dispatch in full, now aimed at a rescue instead of a heist — "get the asset off the dock" — and the gut-punch of catching himself: the asset is a person, the dock is the machine that ate him, and he's the only one with the credential to walk in. NEW range: the dry wit curdles into something almost tender and appalled — *you survived the thing and your first free thought is the entry vector for going back.*
  - Physical expression: His free hand (Beat 1 / Beat 3 callback) opens and closes — reaching for a problem that isn't on his board. He could put it down. He doesn't.
- **DONNA**
  - Subtext: She will NOT push. Pushing would make it her job again, and she's done handling him. She lets him see her SEE it — that he's already reaching.
  - Voice cadence: Cues only. Maybe one word. The restraint IS the respect — first time she treats him fully as a subject, not an asset she deploys.
  - Physical expression: Watches him decide. Doesn't move toward the screen. Lets the gravity do it.
- **COLE**: Set up here — he moves toward the screen, hand near it, NOT yet shoving. The body coiling for Beat 7. He's already decided this one is Russ's.

**Relationship dynamic:** Donna-and-Russ inverted from Movement One: she rescued him; now she refuses to deploy him, leaving the choice fully his — which is its own kind of love and its own kind of weight. **Dialogue direction:** Keep it nearly wordless aloud; the beat lives in Russ's interiority and Donna's restraint. ### Beat 7 — The Name He Can Say (eaten-word INVERSION + the hook)
**Structural summary:** Cole — WORDLESSLY — shoves the screen toward Russ. The single NAME none of them has heard sits there, blinking once, waiting, as it surfaced at Ch14's close. Russ braces for the eraser to eat the name like it eats everything — and it DOESN'T. The name comes whole. He can SAY it. THAT is the terror: a true noun the eraser leaves alone means this one isn't "units," isn't safely dissolved — it's a person his survival-grammar can't protect him from. He begins to say it aloud, committing himself in the saying — and the chapter dies on the dash mid-name.

**Character annotations:**
- **COLE** (the wordless push)
  - Subtext: He banks his OWN word by making RUSS the one who speaks. The shove says *this one's yours* louder than a sentence could. Wordless agency at its peak — he authors the chapter's turn without a syllable.
  - Stated vs. unspoken want: Stated (in body) — *read it.* Unspoken — *I won't spend mine, but you will spend yours, and that's the difference between you and the dead: you can.*
  - Physical expression: The SHOVE — one decisive push of the screen across the bar top. Then stillness. Eyes on Russ, not the screen. The gun re-cocked, not fired. NO WORD. Do not let a sound escape him.
- **RUSS (POV)**
  - Subtext: The inversion lands as dread, not relief. All book, the eraser ate true nouns and he hated it. Now it spares ONE — and the sparing is worse, because a name the eraser won't touch is a name he can't file under "units," can't dissolve, can't keep at handler's-distance. The sayable name drags the person fully real and fully his.
  - HOW to render "a name he can say" as MORE frightening than the names he can't: He BRACES for the familiar dissolve — leans into the eraser the way you lean into an expected stair — and the stair isn't there. The name holds. The horror is the ERASER'S ABSENCE: the safety mechanism that swallowed the cold-room people (kept them abstract, kept him functional) refuses this one. With the dead, the eraser was a mercy he resented; here it withholds the mercy. He can hold this name in his mouth, which means he can't put it down, which means he's going. The whole-ness of the word is the trap closing — from the outside IN.
  - Stated vs. unspoken want: Stated — to say it (and not). Unspoken — for the eraser to eat it, please, let it be units, let me off — and the eraser's refusal is the chapter's verdict: no exit.
  - Voice cadence: Interior — the brace, then the wrongness of the word arriving intact. Then the spoken attempt: PROSE IN QUOTES, the name beginning, cut on the dash. NEW range fused: the dry wit and the dread collapse into one — there is no joke left and he knows it, and the knowing is almost funny in its bleakness, then isn't.
  - Physical expression: CLOSE-RHYME payoff — his FREE hand (Beat 1) reaches, this time to speak a name that will put him back behind a door he'll open from the OUTSIDE IN. Hands that held nothing now reaching to pick up the one thing he can't set down. Narrator never flags the echo.
- **DONNA, MARCO, DEVANG, THEO** (at the edges): Present, watching the screen / the room. Donna's read complete — she knew before he did. Marco/Devang remote, the name surfacing in THEIR feed too (the screen text). Theo at the edge, gone quiet — the over-talker with nothing to say, which is its own alarm (his Beat-2 thinning paying off into silence).
- **THE COLLECTOR** (outside only): His first Movement-Two move rendered as cold machinery — status flip / schedule opening on the SCREEN, no voice, no breath, no gloat. NEW plural-capable menace: render the move so it reads as a MANY acting through one channel — frictionless, simultaneous, the courtesy of a system that no longer has a single throat to aim at. He is scarier now because the crew's "be a many he can't model" trick is dead: he can be the many too. Show this as the move arriving without a seam — nothing to exploit, nowhere the individual shows.

**Relationship dynamic:** Cole AUTHORS the turn (the shove), Russ SPEAKS it (and is consumed by speaking), Donna WITNESSES it (the mirror confirming the math), the collector ENABLES it (faceless gravity). The crew's roles invert from Movement One's rescue: there, they pulled Russ out; here, one of them (Cole) silently pushes him back toward the door — because the new asymmetry might be the one man branded as already-inside.
**Dialogue direction:**

The page must die mid-utterance — the sayable word snatched by the chapter break, so the dread inverts the device: the eaten-word hook lands on the ONE word that wasn't eaten, cut off by the page instead.
