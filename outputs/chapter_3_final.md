# Chapter 3 — The Runbook

The door gave.

It was unlocked — that was the first thing, before the smell, before the table, before any of it. His palm had been flat against it expecting weight, expecting a fight, expecting the locked refusal of a house that did not want him, and instead the latch let go under three pounds of pressure and the door swung in on a quiet hinge, and Cole half-fell across his own braced strength into the kitchen of a man who had texted him *don't come in* and then had not turned the deadbolt. The two facts would not sit together. *He told you not to come in. He didn't lock the door.* A warning with the lock left off is not a warning. It is a thing held open. Cole stood in the doorway with his hand still raised against a door that was no longer there, and the house breathed out at him.

It breathed coffee.

Warm coffee — not the ghost of it, not the stale brown smell of a pot left on a burner since morning, but the actual living steam-rich smell of coffee poured inside the hour, and Cole's whole body understood it before his mind did, the way you understand a hand on your shoulder in the dark. Somebody had been here. Somebody had stood in this room and poured a cup, and the room still held the warmth of them, and the warmth was the most frightening thing he had ever walked into, because warmth meant close, and close meant *minutes,* and minutes meant he had driven a thousand miles to arrive one cup of coffee too late.

He did not call out. The thought of it — *Russ, hey, Russ, you here, hey* — formed and died in his chest without ever reaching his throat. Calling out was speech and speech was gone; it had been gone since yesterday on a tailgate, gone the length of the 405 and the 118, gone the way it always went when the fear got past the place where charm could meet it. So he did the only thing left in him, which was the thing that had carried him this whole long silent day: he moved forward. Six feet of linoleum between him and the table. He crossed it the way he'd crossed the threshold — because there was no road left to drive and no joke left to make and the only direction the body knew anymore was *in.*

And the table was set for one.

He saw it now from the inside, the thing he'd read backward through the glass from the driveway — one plate, one fork, the napkin folded into a clean white square the way you fold a napkin when you have time and nothing else to do with your hands. The laptop, closed, a black slab. And the saltshaker, and under the saltshaker the page, and in his pocket, against his hip, a thousand miles away and an inch from his body, the phone began to buzz.

---

Two thousand five hundred miles east, and four hundred north, and forty-five impossible minutes south, three men were screaming into a thread that had gone dark.

Cole had said *here.* That was the last word. *Here,* and then nothing, for four minutes, for five, the cursor pulsing under it into a white that gave nothing back, and the three of them did with the silence the only thing their nervous systems had ever learned to do with a thing too big to feel.

> theo: cole
> theo: cole baby it's been four minutes
> theo: ok i'm gonna do a tight five while we wait. is this thing on
> theo: knock knock
> theo: ...
> theo: the bit where nobody says who's there is actually killing me right now genuinely

On a Saturday sidewalk in West Hollywood, Theo stood at the curb where he'd walked off his shift an hour ago and never gone back in, and he talked out loud to the empty street between texts because the voice-memo reflex had nowhere left to go. A woman walked her dog past him and he smiled at her, automatic, beautiful, a man whose face did not know how to stop performing even now, and the moment she was gone the smile fell off him like something cut loose.

> marco: Everyone stay organized. Here is where we are.
> marco: 1. Cole is on scene. 2. Cole has been on scene for — stand by — six minutes. 3. Cole has not reported.
> marco: 4. He's inside.
> marco: 4. He's inside and fine.
> marco: 4. He's inside and—

In a commuter parking lot in New Jersey, Marco paced a painted line between two cars with two apps open on a phone he had nothing to put into either of them. He was an archivist with no data, a keeper with nothing to keep; the location dot had stopped and the texts had stopped and there was nothing in the world to organize, so he numbered anyway, the way you say a rosary, the structure standing in for the thing the structure was supposed to hold. The fourth item would not resolve. He kept typing 4 and the 4 kept not finishing.

> devang: ack. logging the silence.
> devang: 90 seconds, no report. now 120.
> devang: a healthy service emits. it sends a heartbeat. you don't have to like the heartbeat, you just have to get one.
> devang: ours has stopped emitting.
> devang: that's not down. down i can work. this is an unknown state and an unknown state is worse than down, i've said that, i'll keep saying it, somebody log that i said it.

Devang whispered it into a baby monitor's worth of nursery quiet, his six-week-old asleep on the play mat in a bar of late northern sun, and he narrated the dead process in the lowest voice he owned because the alternative — to look straight at the fact that the process was his best friend's son standing in his best friend's kitchen not answering — was a thing he could not afford to do while holding it together three feet from a sleeping child. The jargon was a place to stand. He stood on it.

And then, into all of it, the one piece of news that felt like air —

> marco: Update. The welfare unit. I called back and pushed. A unit is NEAR. They're saying minutes.
> theo: oh thank god
> theo: THANK god ok
> devang: a body. an actual body with eyes, on scene. status moving toward green.

— and the three of them grabbed it the way you grab a rope, not one of them knowing that the rope was tied to nothing, that somewhere in a house they could not see a page under a saltshaker said the one word that turned the cavalry into the thing it was riding toward.

---

In the house, Cole lifted the saltshaker.

He did it carefully, the way you lift a thing in a room where everything is a held breath, two fingers, set it down on the bare wood beside the plate without a sound. The page came free. It was a single sheet, white, gone soft at one corner from being handled, and the writing on it was Russ's — Cole knew the hand before he knew the words, the blunt deliberate print of a man who filled out work orders, who wrote *PAID* and *FOLLOW UP* in the margins of invoices, who had never in his life written a sentence he could not have said to your face.

Except this one. These ones.

> *whoever finds this — if you're reading it then it went how i thought it would.*
> *don't call the police. i mean that. no cops. it makes it worse and i can't put why on a piece of paper, you just have to trust me, no cops.*
> *spare key isn't under the rock. i moved it. it's taped inside the meter box, left side. furnace filter's overdue — there's two new ones on the shelf above the water heater, the 16x25, low and slow on the slide or you'll bend the frame.*
> *donna. go to your sister's. take the baby and go to your sister's like i said and don't come back to the house. i had it handled. i just didn't. i'm sorry it's like this. i'll fix it.*

Cole read it the way you read a thing you already half-know — fast and then slow, the words going in before the sense did, his eye snagging on *donna* the way a sleeve snags on a nail. He read it again. And the second time through, the floor changed shape under his feet while his hand was still flat on the table.

Because there was no goodbye in it. There was no *I love you* and no *tell the guys* and no *it's not your fault.* There was a moved key and a furnace filter and *low and slow* and *I'll fix it,* and a dead man does not leave instructions for the next furnace filter, a dead man does not write *I'll fix it,* a dead man does not tell his wife which sister to drive to. This was not a note. It was a *runbook.* He had not finished. He had *planned.*

And it was addressed to Donna. *Donna, go to your sister's, like I said.* He'd written it for Donna to find. He'd thought Donna would be the one standing here.

Donna was not standing here. Cole was.

---

The DM surfaced in his memory whole and lit, the way a thing you didn't understand the first time arrives the second time already understanding itself. *don't come in. i'm not there. it's not what you think. call donna NOW —*

It was not a goodbye either. It had never been a goodbye. *I'm not there* — because he was somewhere else, alive, moving, in a van that the others still hadn't thought to ask about. *It's not what you think* — because they all thought the worst thing, and the worst thing was a misdirect, a costume, a set table and an apology built to be read exactly the way they'd read it, so that the danger would follow the read and not the man. And *call donna NOW* — because the plan had a hole in it, and the hole was that Donna was supposed to be here and Cole was here instead. Russ, alive, watching, *steering* — the dropped pin, the *don't stop texting me,* a man who could see Cole's blue dot crawling north and had spent his own catastrophe getting there first one last time, making the kid's panic smaller from inside his own. Russ had aimed Cole at this door on purpose, to read this page, to make the one call the page could not make itself.

The money — the lien, the second mortgage, the lapsed loan Devang had found — a wall of money does not write *no cops.* A bank does not make you move your wife to her sister's. This kind of debt did not come with a payment plan. It came with a person. *I'll fix it* was a man walking toward a person to keep the person away from a house with a baby in it.

His thumb went to the Chain. Pure reflex — the Chain was home, the Chain was where everything went, every fever and every score and every fear of his whole adult life. He started to type. *hes ali* — three letters and the start of a fourth, his thumb already flooding the way it always flooded —

— and he stopped. And deleted it.

Because he knew, with a clarity he had never once had about anything, what the Chain would do with this. Theo would reflex-joke before he could stop himself — not cruelty, just the only sound those men knew how to make when they were too scared to be sincere — and Russ's no-cops, Russ's moved key, Russ's whole agonized engineering to keep this off the people he loved could not become a bit. Not this one. For once in his life Cole protected the thing instead of laughing at it.

He thumbed past the thread to the dialer. He found the number Marco had been throwing into the void all day, the one nobody had reached, and he pressed it, and he put the phone to his ear, and the man whose entire self had been the easy charm of words stood in a stranger's kitchen with no words at all and listened to a line begin to ring.

---

It rang.

And while it rang, his eye caught the wrong thing.

The coffee was warm. He could still smell it, living and close. But the truck — the truck out front had been ticking when he pulled in, that slow *tink, tink* of a block that had stopped a while ago, the truck that had been sitting cooling since before he'd even crossed Simi, and a cup of coffee does not stay warm as long as a truck stays warm, the two clocks did not run at the same speed, the two clocks did not *agree* — and there, by the sink, a second cup. Ringed inside with a cold brown tideline. Two cups. One warm, one not. Someone had been here after. Or someone had been here with —

The thought arrived and his overloaded mind could not catch it; it went past him like a face in a passing car, *that's wrong, that doesn't fit a man gone minutes ago,* and then the line connected and blew it clean out of his head.

> theo: THE CAVALRY. baby i told you. a unit on scene, eyes on, this is the part where it turns around
> marco: Unit arriving. Finally. 1. Eyes. 2. A door. 3. A human being who can SEE.
> devang: status: green. first green log in—

A car door, somewhere off-page, on a street none of them could see. The cops Russ's page had begged him not to call, pulling up to a house with a runbook on the table that said *no cops* in a dead man's living hand, and the three blind men a thousand miles out cheering the cavalry over the cliff.

The phone clicked. A breath on the line. Alive — a person, alive, breathing — and then a voice, plain and frightened and nothing at all like the Chain, the first voice from this side of the country in three chapters, no joke in it and no jargon and no stretched vowel, only a real woman bracing for the worst news of her life.

"Hello?"

Everything Cole had not said all day stood up behind his teeth at once. *omg donna hi its cole im russ's friend somethings happening but hes okay i think hes okay theres a note and it says* — the whole comma-less flood, the torrent that would have filled a screen in a heartbeat, surged up into his throat and jammed there, because aloud, to a real frightened woman, each word cost a hundred times what typing had ever cost, because saying it would make it real, and the man whose whole life had been the effortless charm of words discovered, standing in the wreck of his best friend's plan, that he had none.

"Donna." The only word he was sure of.

"It's—" His own name would not come. *It's Cole* — too big, somehow, too much to be a person right now. He let it go.

"I'm. At the house. I'm at the house."

And then the one true thing, the thing the page had demanded, smaller than any text he had ever sent — he reached for *alive,* and it wouldn't come, and he reached for *gone,* and it wouldn't come either, and what came out of him was wrong-shaped and borrowed, the dead man's own words secondhand in the son's mouth:

"He's— ...he's not here."

A sound on the line. An indrawn breath that was almost a word.

"Cole?" His name, in her mouth, like she was placing it from a long way off. "Cole, oh my god — where are you, are you— is he with you? Did you find— no. No, you said the house, you're at the *house* —" and her voice changed, went sharp, the decades of being handled rising in it all at once, a woman feeling the board tilt under pieces she hadn't known she was one of. "You can't be at the house. Cole, you can't be there, who— *who told you to go there?* Who *told* you—"

And it cut.

Mid-word. Mid-breath. On *told.* The line went to a flat dead nothing against his ear, the way a screen goes white under a sentence that never finished, the answer arriving in a human voice for the first time in three days and snatched away in the same breath it came, and behind Cole, on the wall above the table set for one, a light began to turn — red, then nothing, then red — a slow rotating wash thrown through the kitchen window from a car that had just pulled up to a house it had been told not to come to.

Cole stood in the turning light with a dead phone against one ear and a dead man's runbook in his other hand, the unlocked door open behind him, the unspoken thing finally spoken, and not one thing in the world made safer for it.
