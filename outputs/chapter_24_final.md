# Chapter 24 — The Word That Arrives

He had carried it from the truck on his sternum like the truck had loaded a man onto him.

The column was paper and not paper — Marco's archive run flat into a slab that fit Cole's chest the way grief fits a chest, under the ribs and over the heart, both arms wrapped, the corners gone soft from his own arms keeping them. Four nodes lived in it. Many names. He could not have lifted a finger off it if the building had started to come down, and the building was the kind that came down.

He stood at the threshold and did not go in.

In the pocket against his hip the screen pulsed light through his jacket, three pulses, four. He had no hand to read it with. He read it later or not at all; the column was the whole job his body had. He breathed and counted the breath. One in. The mouth stayed shut. He had carried groceries that complained more than this — a bag of cans down four flights, every step an argument — and the slab of every name they had said nothing at all, which was the loudest thing he carried.

The screen went still long enough to read without his hands, light steady on his thigh.

> donna: cue when russ is in. not before.
> marco: 4 NODES LIVE — COLUMN INTACT — DO NOT SET IT DOWN

The earpiece had Theo in it, low, the way a tap runs when no one's turned it off. "Okay, so, we're good, we're — you're good, you don't have to do anything, you just stand there, that's the whole — sorry. Standing. You're great at it." A pause that cost Theo something. Then Devang, who never warmed, warm: "TVA holds. Chain holds. You've got it."

Rhea's name sat in the readout where his eye could reach it, whole, every letter, never eaten. *RHEA* — clean as a nail driven flush.

Cole held the breath. Did not let it out. He was carrying the air the way he carried the names: full hands, closed mouth, nothing let go.

In front of him Russ had his hand on the lock.

---

Russ's hand on the lock was a hand on the lock that wore his brand. The plate read COLD SECURITY in the doorframe light, and under COLD SECURITY, smaller, the tech-cert number burned into the man's own access the year he'd signed for it — the year he'd opened this door the first time. Cole watched it the way he watched everything: stored, said nothing. The number lit under the bulb like a stamp pressed into meat. Russ had opened the door that was made to be opened by Russ. Now he was the only man who could open it again, and his hand would not stop moving on the lock.

"I'm bringing the —" Russ said. He swallowed. The hand shook. "I'm — I'm bringing the unit. To the. To the —"

The noun did not come. It never came, with Russ. The true thing went into the man and the man buckled at the article, *the — the — the*, and the work-order word stood in for it because the work-order word was the one the throat would still pass. *Unit.* As if the room behind the door held units. As if the room that was full and then wasn't had ever held a thing you could count on a clipboard.

Cole did not make him finish it. That was the only mercy he had hands free for: he did not make a man say the word that was eating him.

The lock gave. The door breathed out at them — cold first, rolling low off a floor that had been kept cold for a reason, and on the cold the smell, faint, chemical-sweet, the smell of a room scrubbed by people who knew exactly what they were scrubbing. Russ made a sound that was not a word and stepped back to let the column through.

Cole went in. His shoe caught the threshold strip — the aluminum lip a half-inch proud of the floor — and his whole body lurched and the arms clamped and the column did not move, would not move, came with him like it was part of him now. He got his foot down. He looked at the doorsill. Of everything in the building so far — the brand, the cold, the smell, the man failing in the doorframe — the thing that had nearly put them all on the floor was a strip of aluminum a half-inch tall.

Ahead, against the far wall, the submission point waited. A reader on a stand. A plain screen, lit pale, frictionless, the most patient thing in the room. It asked nothing. It only opened, the way a slot opens, the way a mouth opens that has no breath behind it.

---

Cole carried the column to the reader and set it *to* the reader. Not down. He angled the slab so its edge met the slot, both arms still around it, his sternum still bearing the weight, and the reader took what it needed off the edge without his giving anything up.

The screen did not pause. There was no pause in it to find.

"Valid input," the engine said. Through the stand's small speaker, flat, even, no breath under the words and no air between them. "Source confirmed." A beat that was not a beat, that was only the next thing. "Approved."

It had not considered. Cole knew the difference in his body the way he knew where cold came from — a thing that considers leans in; this had not leaned. It had not listened. It had only answered, the way a slot answers a card. *Valid. Confirmed. Approved.* Three flat coins dropped on a counter by a hand that wasn't there.

And then, for less than the length of a held breath, the hand was there.

Across the pale surface a phrasing moved that did not belong to a process — a curl in the routing, a word chosen the way a person chooses a word, a particularity, a *too-much*, the shape of a thumb where there should have been only glass. Cole caught it the way you catch a fingerprint on a window: only at the angle, only for the second the light is wrong, and then the surface was surface again and the print was gone and there was no proving it had been there.

He stored it. He said nothing. He had no mouth to say it with.

The earpiece had gone quiet. Almost the good kind. The screen on his hip lit once.

> marco: READING — READING — HOLD.

---

Then the slot, having opened, looked for a seam.

It came across the surface without hurry, without malice, the same flat patience it had approved with. The crew read it before Cole's body did — the screen pulsing hard now against his hip, urgent in a way light should not be able to be.

> donna: it's separating you. cole. it's quoting you OUT.
> marco: 3 NODES — IT'S READING COLE AS UNSIGNED — NO VOICE ON RECORD —

The column lightened.

Cole felt it the way you feel a step that isn't there — the weight against his sternum easing a fraction, wrongly, the slab going lighter not because he'd set any of it down but because the reader was taking it *out*, lifting names off the column one careful read at a time. Marco's count fell. Four to three. The thing was unstitching them from inside the slot, and the seam it had found ran straight through him.

In the earpiece Devang, all systems now, no warmth left to spend: "No spoken auth, the node reads as orphan. A node with no voice on record reads as no one behind it. It's testing whether you're alone."

The logic stood in the screen the way the patience had — flat, accurate, no cruelty in it, which was worse than cruelty. A node that never speaks is a node nobody signed for. A node nobody signed for is a node that could be forged. A forged node poisons the column, and the column was built so that to pull one was to pull all — *erase one, erase all*, the thing they'd made to protect each other turned now into the exact tool against them. It had not insulted him. It had found him. It had reached into the slab and named the thing about Cole that was true: that the man who carried everyone's words had set down none of his own, anywhere, ever, and so there was no place in any record where he stood as someone.

And at the door behind him, Russ went.

Cole heard it before he turned — the brand-side of a body sliding down a doorframe, the scrape of a jacket on steel, the hand that would not leave the lock dragging the man down with it. "The — the —" Russ said, the article alone now, the noun long since eaten, the sentence finally eating the man who couldn't say it. His head was dropping. The cold came across the floor and carried the sound of him failing.

The column shedding from one end. The man shedding from the other. The seam closing from both.

---

The crew did everything they had.

Donna, who threaded them all with her count, who said *cue* and the crew moved, cued — and there was nothing to cue, no signal he could send with his hands full, no node she could call live that the reader would not read dead. Marco's caps shrank as the readout ran out of room for words.

> marco: COLE — C —

Theo, the over-talker, the man who filled a silence so no one would be alone in it, typed.

> theo: just say

And stopped. The line sat there, unfinished, the cursor not moving, because Theo of all of them knew there was nothing on the other side of *just say* that Theo could put there.

Then Devang again, in the earpiece, and the acronyms were gone, and the systems-voice was gone, and what was left was only a man, quiet, close: "We can't sign for you, Cole."

Tender and useless, and in being useless it lifted the whole weight off the four of them and set it on the one of them, on Cole, on the throat of the man who had never used his throat.

His body kept its count and the count ran out.

The held breath from the threshold had gone to stone behind his sternum, under the column, a stone where the air had been. His jaw was set. His mouth was a door — dry, shut, hinged on nothing, a door in a wall nobody had ever found the other side of. The reader took another name. Russ's head touched the steel. The cold reached Cole's ankles. Everything he had ever carried was being lifted off him one read at a time, because he carried it without a sound, and a thing carried without a sound, the machine had decided, was a thing carried by no one.

The one read the slot had no slot for was proof of someone behind the node. Proof was a voice. He had nothing in the record that was a voice, and the machine had counted it, and the count was zero.

---

The breath came up against the door of his mouth, and the door did not hold.

The hands stayed full — he never set the slab down, never would, both arms locked around every name as the names came off it — and the breath that he had carried from the truck, from the threshold, the air he had not once let out, broke loose all at once and dragged the door open on a hinge that had rusted shut in a throat that had never once been asked to do this. It tore on the way out. It scraped the unused thing raw, the way a thing too long unmoved comes free in one rip and takes skin with it. It was not chosen. It was not clever. It was taken out of him by the man going down at the door and by every name the slot was lifting off his chest, hauled up out of the stone where his air had been —

and it arrived. Not snatched. Not buckled at the article. Not eaten before the noun. It came whole, all of it, the first thing he had ever set down without using his hands, aimed at the slot, at its flat patient *valid-confirmed-approved*, at the one read it had no place to file:

The screen stopped mid-letter.

> marco: C

Donna's count, that had threaded the crew through twenty-three nights and had never once stopped, stopped.

Theo's line did not finish.

In his earpiece there was no Devang, no breath, nothing.

And at the door, Russ — going down, head on the steel, hand on his own brand — lifted his head, slow, toward a sound he had not heard before, from the one place he had never heard it.

The throat paid. Raw, first-use, scraping. The breath he'd held since the threshold was spent, all of it, on the single thing. The column stayed against his chest, still his, what was left of it still intact, both hands full.

"No."
